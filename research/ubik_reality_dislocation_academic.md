## 2. Short Abstract

This article proposes Philip K. Dick’s *Ubik* as a central literary model for understanding the contemporary dislocation of reality under conditions of increasing complexity, platform mediation, synthetic media, and generative artificial intelligence. Rather than treating *Ubik* merely as a novel of illusion or paranoia, the paper reads it as a work about the weakening of the practical procedures through which a shared reality is stabilized.

The article introduces the concept of the “Ubik effect”: the subjective and collective experience of reality dislocation that occurs when signs, objects, messages, institutions, identities, and temporal frames lose stable provenance or operational consistency. It then contrasts several possible responses to this dislocation: Orwellian authoritarian stabilization through trace control; Wellsian exploration of possible futures; Scottian figures of capture and artificial life; and the persona problem in Stevenson, Pirandello, and Borges.

The article argues that the age of AI does not merely require improved fact-checking. It requires procedural stabilizers: provenance, versioning, source corpora, traceability, explicit personas, qualified objections, contextual certification, review procedures, and return-to-corpus mechanisms. In this sense, the paper frames “procedural Ubik” as a democratic alternative to magical, authoritarian, commercial, or algorithmic stabilizers.

## 4. Research Question

How can Philip K. Dick’s *Ubik* help us understand the contemporary dislocation of shared reality under conditions of complexity, platform mediation, and generative artificial intelligence?

Secondary questions:

1. What distinguishes reality dislocation from illusion, falsehood, or simulation?
2. How does *Ubik* stage the weakening of the procedures that allow reality to hold?
3. How do other works of science fiction and speculative literature illuminate alternative responses to reality dislocation?
4. Why is fact-checking insufficient as a response to AI-generated uncertainty?
5. What would a democratic, procedural stabilizer of reality look like?

---

## 5. Central Hypothesis

The central hypothesis is:

> Contemporary complexity does not destroy reality. It dislocates the practical conditions through which reality can be stabilized as a shared world.

This leads to a second hypothesis:

> The age of generative AI requires not only tools for detecting falsehood, but infrastructures for maintaining provenance, traceability, source/form/platform distinctions, explicit personas, qualified objections, and correction procedures.

---

## 6. Main Contribution

The article contributes:

1. A new interpretive reading of *Ubik* as a model of reality dislocation rather than merely illusion or paranoia.
2. The concept of the **Ubik effect** as a diagnostic category for contemporary mediated reality.
3. A distinction between four types of stabilizers:
   - magical stabilizers;
   - authoritarian stabilizers;
   - commercial / platform stabilizers;
   - procedural / democratic stabilizers.
4. A bridge between science-fiction studies, media theory, AI governance, and epistemology.
5. A conceptual foundation for **procedural Ubik** as a democratic response to AI-mediated uncertainty.

## 9. Proposed Structure

### Introduction — Why *Ubik* Now?

- Open with the contemporary difficulty of stabilizing reality.
- Introduce AI, synthetic media, platform mediation, institutional distrust, and complexity.
- Present *Ubik* as central, not illustrative.
- State the research question and contribution.

### Section 1 — Reading *Ubik* as Reality Dislocation

- Distinguish illusion from dislocation.
- Analyze regression of objects, uncertain messages, half-life, and unstable levels of reality.
- Define the Ubik effect.
- Interpret Ubik as fantasy of stabilization.

### Section 2 — Complexity and the Cost of Reality Maintenance

- Explain why complex systems generate orientation fatigue.
- Show that too much mediated information can produce dislocation.
- Introduce source/form/platform confusion.
- Link to synthetic media and generative AI.

### Section 3 — Stabilizers: Magical, Authoritarian, Commercial, Procedural

- Ubik as magical stabilizer.
- Orwell as authoritarian stabilizer.
- Platforms and markets as commercial stabilizers.
- Procedural stabilization as democratic alternative.

### Section 4 — Satellite Works and Regimes of Appearance

- Wells: exploration instead of closure.
- *The War of the Worlds*: abstract intelligence and the living milieu.
- Scott’s *Alien*: capture from within the system.
- *Blade Runner*: the right to appear as living.
- Shelley: creation and responsibility.
- Stevenson: persona capture.
- Pirandello and Borges: plural selves and public capture.

### Section 5 — AI and the Intensification of the Ubik Effect

- AI as machine for producing appearances.
- Texts without stable authorship.
- Images without cameras.
- Voices without bodies.
- Personas without biography.
- Answers without provenance.
- Explain why fact-checking is insufficient.

### Section 6 — Toward a Procedural Ubik

- Define procedural Ubik.
- Core mechanisms:
  - provenance;
  - versioning;
  - source corpora;
  - traceability;
  - explicit personas;
  - contextual certification;
  - qualified objections;
  - review procedures;
  - correction without erasure;
  - return to corpus.
- Link to democratic governance and AI safety.

### Conclusion — Making Reality Hold

- Restate that reality has not disappeared.
- The question is how to preserve conditions of shared reality.
- Reject magical, authoritarian, and purely commercial stabilizers.
- Conclude with procedural stabilization as democratic necessity.

## 14. Risks and Points to Control

### Risk 1 — Overextension

The article may cover too many works.

Mitigation:

- Keep *Ubik* central.
- Treat other works as satellites, not equal objects.

### Risk 2 — Conceptual inflation

“Procedural Ubik” may sound too metaphorical.

Mitigation:

- Define it precisely.
- Link it to concrete mechanisms: provenance, versioning, traceability, review, correction.

### Risk 3 — Weak academic anchoring

The article may read as an essay if theoretical references remain too loose.

Mitigation:

- Add a compact but serious literature review.
- Clearly position the contribution.

### Risk 4 — AI governance drift

The article may drift away from literature into policy.

Mitigation:

### Risk 5 — French / English positioning

## 17. Closing Formula

The academic article should demonstrate that *Ubik* is not merely a novel about unstable reality.

It is a literary machine for perceiving a contemporary condition:

> a world where reality still exists, but the procedures that allow it to hold as a common world are increasingly fragile.

The task is not to replace uncertainty with authority.

The task is to build procedural stabilizers that make reality traceable, contestable, corrigible, and shareable.
