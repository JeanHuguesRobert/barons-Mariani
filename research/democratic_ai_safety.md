# Democratic AI Safety

## Why AI Safety Must Protect Human Sovereignty Against AI-Augmented Legal Persons

**Jean Hugues Noël Robert, baron Mariani**  
Institut Mariani / C.O.R.S.I.C.A.  
Corte, Corsica, France

*Working paper — May 2026 — draft v0.4*

---

## Note to the Reader

This text is a working paper and a thesis kernel. It does not claim final proof. It proposes a structured argument, makes its central claims explicit, anticipates objections, and states what could weaken or falsify the argument.

The purpose is not to close debate, but to make the debate more navigable.

This draft reduces internal jargon, adds contemporary signals, and engages more directly with existing AI Safety and AI governance frameworks. It does not argue that technical AI Safety is wrong. It argues that technical AI Safety is **layer-incomplete** if it lacks an explicit theory of AI-augmented institutional agency.

---

## Abstract

Current AI Safety frameworks primarily focus on technical alignment, model evaluations, frontier capability thresholds, misuse risks, human oversight, and deployment governance. These approaches are necessary, but incomplete. They tend to underemphasize a political pathway of AI risk: artificial intelligence may not first become sovereign by itself; it may instead amplify existing non-human legal persons — corporations, platforms, funds, bureaucracies, and other institutions — into de facto sovereign actors.

The corporation is the legal precedent. It is already a non-human person capable of owning property, contracting, litigating, lobbying, accumulating capital, and outliving natural persons. AI may add speed, persuasion, optimization, surveillance, legal automation, synthetic media, and agentic execution. The resulting danger is not merely “unsafe AI”, but **AI-augmented corporate agency**.

This paper argues that democratic AI Safety must therefore address the risk of **human political obsolescence**. The relevant existential question is not only whether machines may replace humans physically, but whether humans may cease to be the effective subjects of collective decision-making.

The refusal of patent offices and courts to recognize AI systems as inventors provides an unintended but useful principle: AI may assist, but legally opposable knowledge still requires human responsibility. If this is true for private patents, it should be considered at least relevant for public knowledge, democratic deliberation, and the Commons.

DHITL — Democratic Human-in-the-Loop — is proposed as a political extension of human oversight. AI may assist citizens, deliberation, memory, critique, and public reasoning. It must not transform non-human legal or digital persons into members of the demos.

Cogentia Commons and PrivAI are proposed as minimal institutional responses: Cogentia Commons preserves accountable public knowledge; PrivAI protects private cognitive sovereignty. Together, they aim to ensure that AI accelerates human collective intelligence without making human citizens politically obsolete.

## 0. Method Status

This paper distinguishes three levels of assertion:

| Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Established fact** | Supported by legal texts, institutional documents, or broadly verifiable public records. |
| **Analytical hypothesis** | A reasoned inference from observed structures and current trends. |
| **Normative doctrine** | A proposed principle for democratic AI governance. |

The central thesis is not presented as a proven inevitability. It is presented as a plausible, under-addressed AI Safety pathway requiring institutional design.

## 1. Core Claims

### C1 — AI Safety is incomplete without political agency analysis

Current AI Safety frameworks focus heavily on models, developers, deployment risk, technical alignment, evaluations, and human oversight. This is necessary, but insufficient if the analysis does not include the political agency of AI-augmented institutions.

**Status:** analytical claim.

**Potential falsifier:** a systematic review showing that major AI Safety frameworks already include enforceable mechanisms preventing AI-augmented legal persons from capturing democratic decision-making.

---

### C2 — Legal persons are already non-human actors with political effects

Corporations, foundations, funds, associations, and similar legal persons are non-human entities capable of owning assets, contracting, litigating, lobbying, and accumulating long-term power.

**Status:** established legal-political fact.

**Potential falsifier:** none for the basic legal fact; contestable only in its political interpretation.

---

### C3 — AI may increase the political capacity of legal persons

AI systems may increase the strategic, persuasive, legal, informational, and operational capacities of corporations and other legal persons.

**Status:** analytical hypothesis.

**Potential falsifier:** empirical evidence showing that AI capabilities primarily decentralize power toward natural persons and do not significantly increase the political influence of already powerful legal persons.

---

### C4 — Human political obsolescence is a plausible AI Safety pathway

Human beings may remain formally enfranchised while becoming less effective as subjects of collective decision-making if AI-augmented legal persons dominate law, markets, attention, institutions, and public belief.

**Status:** analytical hypothesis.

**Potential falsifier:** evidence that democratic institutions can reliably constrain AI-augmented legal persons and preserve meaningful citizen sovereignty under high levels of AI deployment.

---

### C5 — Human inventorship doctrine provides a useful symmetry argument

If legal systems require human responsibility when AI-assisted knowledge becomes private property, then public knowledge and democratic deliberation should not exclude human responsibility.

**Status:** legal analogy and normative inference.

**Potential falsifier:** a convincing legal-political theory explaining why human responsibility is required for private patent rights but unnecessary for public knowledge, Commons governance, or democratic decisions.

---

### C6 — Digital personhood must not imply political personhood

Even if future law creates limited digital legal persons for liability, registration, or containment, such entities must never become citizens, voters, or members of the demos.

**Status:** normative doctrine.

**Potential falsifier:** not empirically falsifiable in the strict sense; contestable by democratic constitutional argument.

---

### C7 — Democratic AI Safety requires democratic humans in the loop

Human oversight is insufficient if the human in the loop is merely an economically subordinated operator. Public AI-assisted knowledge and decision-making require accountable democratic human contributors.

**Status:** normative doctrine grounded in political theory.

**Potential falsifier:** a robust model of AI governance showing that non-democratic oversight reliably preserves democratic sovereignty without accountable citizen participation.

## 2. Introduction: The Missing Political Layer of AI Safety

AI Safety is usually framed as the problem of ensuring that artificial intelligence systems behave safely, reliably, and in accordance with human intentions or values.

This framing is useful, but incomplete.

The central question is not only:

> Can we make AI systems safe?

It is also:

> Safe for whom, governed by whom, and aligned with which form of human authority?

A model may be technically aligned with the objectives of its deployer while being politically dangerous for the public. A system may be reliable, controllable, and profitable while serving structures that reduce democratic sovereignty.

This paper argues that a major democratic AI Safety risk is not necessarily the sudden emergence of an autonomous machine sovereign. It is the acceleration of existing non-human legal persons — corporations, platforms, financial entities, bureaucracies — by AI systems that increase their ability to shape law, markets, attention, institutions, and public belief.

The human species may not become physically obsolete first.

It may become politically obsolete.

This is not asserted as an inevitability. It is proposed as a plausible pathway requiring explicit analysis.

## 3. The Dominant AI Safety Paradigm

Dominant AI Safety approaches can be grouped into several families:

| Approach | Central concern |
|---|---|
| Technical alignment | Make AI models follow human intentions or values. |
| Frontier risk management | Identify dangerous capabilities before deployment. |
| Model evaluations | Measure cyber, bio, persuasion, autonomy, or misuse risks. |
| Responsible scaling | Condition deployment on capability and risk thresholds. |
| Human oversight | Ensure humans can monitor, intervene, or stop systems. |
| AI governance | Regulate providers, deployers, and high-risk uses. |
| Responsible AI | Manage bias, robustness, transparency, accountability, and privacy. |

These approaches are necessary.

OpenAI's Preparedness Framework focuses on measuring and mitigating severe harm from frontier AI capabilities. Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy is a voluntary framework for managing catastrophic risks from advanced AI systems. Public AI safety institutes and government evaluation programs increasingly test frontier systems before release, including for cyber, biological, and national security risks.

European regulation also emphasizes human oversight. Article 14 of the EU AI Act states that high-risk AI systems must be designed and developed so they can be effectively overseen by natural persons during use, with the aim of preventing or minimizing risks to health, safety, and fundamental rights.

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework organizes risk management around govern, map, measure, and manage functions for AI risks to individuals, organizations, and society.

These frameworks are valuable.

But they generally ask:

> How do we control AI systems?

This paper asks:

> Who becomes more powerful because AI systems are controlled by them?

That is a different question.

---

## 4. Where Existing Frameworks Fall Short

This paper does not claim that existing AI Safety frameworks are wrong. It claims that they are incomplete at the political-agency layer.

| Framework | What it addresses | What remains under-addressed |
|---|---|---|
| EU AI Act | Risk classification, high-risk obligations, human oversight, transparency, enforcement duties. | Whether AI-augmented legal persons become de facto political actors. |
| NIST AI RMF | Organizational AI risk management through govern, map, measure, and manage functions. | Whether the organization itself becomes too powerful when AI-augmented. |
| Frontier preparedness frameworks | Severe harm risks from model capabilities such as cyber, bio, persuasion, autonomy, or misuse. | Whether safe models can still amplify undemocratic legal persons. |
| Corporate Responsible AI | Internal accountability, safety processes, model governance, user protection. | Whether corporate alignment can conflict with democratic alignment. |
| Human oversight rules | Natural-person monitoring, intervention, and risk reduction. | Whether the human is a democratic actor or a subordinate agent of a legal person. |

The missing layer is not technical.

It is constitutional.

A technically controlled AI system may still be politically unsafe if it is controlled by a non-democratic institution whose interests diverge from the demos.

## 5. Contemporary Signals of AI-Augmented Institutional Power

This section does not claim to prove human political obsolescence. It identifies contemporary signals that make the risk plausible and worth studying.

### 5.1 AI industry lobbying and political spending

AI companies and large technology firms are increasingly active in policy influence. Public reporting has documented rising lobbying expenditures by OpenAI, Anthropic, and other major AI-related firms, as well as the emergence of AI-related political action efforts.

This matters because AI firms are not only building technical systems. They are also shaping the legal environment in which those systems will be governed.

**Signal:** AI governance is already a field of organized institutional influence, not only a technical field.

---

### 5.2 Pressure to weaken or simplify AI regulation

In May 2026, Reuters reported that CEOs from major European technology companies — including ASML, Airbus, Ericsson, Mistral AI, Nokia, SAP, and Siemens — called for EU AI regulations to be reduced and simplified. The appeal came as the EU prepared to resume talks on AI Act implementation and a broader Tech Sovereignty Package.

This does not prove malign intent. Industrial actors may have legitimate concerns about fragmentation, competitiveness, and administrative burden.

But it illustrates the structural issue: major firms are organized, resourced, and positioned to shape the regulatory terrain.

**Signal:** AI-affected legal persons act collectively to influence AI governance.

---

### 5.3 Big Tech influence over AI standards

Civil-society and transparency organizations have warned that major technology firms have significant influence over AI standards and implementation processes in Europe, including standards related to the AI Act.

Such reports should be read critically, especially when produced by advocacy organizations. But they are relevant as warning signals: standard-setting is a technical process with political consequences.

**Signal:** technical standards may become a site where corporate agency shapes democratic constraints.

---

### 5.4 Platform control over AI access

In 2026, Meta contested potential EU measures requiring it to allow rival AI chatbots access to WhatsApp. The case illustrates a broader issue: dominant platforms may control the channels through which AI agents reach users, businesses, and public discourse.

Whether Meta's position is legally justified is not the point here.

The relevant signal is that platform control and AI agent distribution are converging.

**Signal:** whoever controls communication infrastructure may also control AI-mediated agency.

---

### 5.5 Cloud partnerships and sovereignty concerns

Microsoft's partnership with Mistral AI drew EU scrutiny in 2024 because it raised questions about concentration of power, cloud dependency, and the relationship between European AI sovereignty and American infrastructure.

Again, this does not prove capture. Partnerships may be commercially rational and technically useful.

But it illustrates a recurring structural tension: AI sovereignty may depend on infrastructure controlled by legal persons whose strategic interests are not identical to those of the public.

**Signal:** AI sovereignty is not only about models; it is also about legal persons controlling infrastructure.

## 6. The Legal Person as Precedent

The corporation, association, foundation, fund, and similar entities are legal fictions. They are not human beings, yet they can own property, contract, litigate, employ people, lobby governments, accumulate capital, and persist beyond the lifespan of any natural person.

The legal person is therefore the historical precedent for non-human agency inside human law.

This fiction was created for practical reasons. It allows collective activity, economic coordination, long-term investment, and institutional continuity.

But it also produces a structural risk: legal persons may accumulate power without possessing the human qualities that justify democratic sovereignty.

A corporation can be wealthy, durable, strategically rational, and politically influential. It can also externalize harms, diffuse responsibility, outlive victims, and act through layers of representation.

The personhood of corporations is therefore politically ambiguous.

They are persons for economic purposes.

They are not citizens.

They do not vote directly.

Yet they can influence the conditions under which citizens vote.

This ambiguity becomes more dangerous if legal persons are augmented by AI.

## 7. AI-Augmented Legal Persons

AI does not need to become a sovereign subject in order to transform politics.

It may amplify already powerful non-human legal persons.

An AI-augmented corporation may gain:

- continuous strategic analysis;
- automated legal drafting;
- market prediction;
- lobbying optimization;
- individualized persuasion;
- synthetic media production;
- customer and citizen profiling;
- automated negotiation;
- regulatory arbitrage;
- intellectual property acceleration;
- surveillance and sentiment analysis;
- agentic execution across platforms;
- and extreme organizational speed.

This creates a possible new political actor:

> the AI-augmented legal person.

Such an actor is not a human being. It is not a member of the demos. Yet it may become more capable than citizens and democratic institutions at shaping the environment in which public decisions are made.

The central danger is not necessarily that AI alone becomes a political subject.

The danger is that corporations, platforms, and other legal persons become machine-accelerated subjects of de facto sovereignty.

---

## 8. Human Political Obsolescence

The classical existential-risk narrative often imagines AI systems physically eliminating or replacing humans.

This paper proposes a more immediate political scenario:

> humans remain alive, but cease to be the effective subjects of collective decision-making.

Human political obsolescence may occur when:

- citizens formally retain rights;
- elections formally continue;
- institutions formally remain democratic;
- but the real capacity to shape attention, law, policy, markets, norms, and belief shifts toward AI-augmented non-human entities.

This scenario requires no conscious AI.

It requires only:

- concentrated capital;
- legal personhood;
- platform infrastructure;
- asymmetry of information;
- automated persuasion;
- strategic optimization;
- and weak democratic counter-infrastructure.

The resulting system may still call itself democratic.

But the demos no longer governs effectively.

This is the core democratic AI Safety concern.

---

## 9. Human Inventorship as an Unintended Democratic Precedent

Patent law provides an important clue.

In several jurisdictions, AI systems are not recognized as inventors. Patent systems continue to require a human inventor, even when AI assists the inventive process.

The United States Patent and Trademark Office states that AI systems, including generative AI and computational models, are tools used by human inventors; such tools do not qualify as inventors. The European Patent Office's DABUS decisions similarly held that a machine is not an inventor within the meaning of the European Patent Convention.

The immediate purpose of these positions is not democratic theory. It is patent doctrine.

But the consequence is broader.

The law is saying:

> AI may assist, but the legally opposable act must remain anchored in human responsibility.

This creates a symmetry argument.

If a human is required when AI-assisted knowledge becomes private property, then a human should be required when AI-assisted knowledge enters the public Commons.

It would be incoherent to require a human when knowledge becomes a patent, but exclude the human when knowledge becomes public truth, public reasoning, or democratic decision.

The private sphere cannot demand human responsibility for ownership while the public sphere abandons human responsibility for the Commons.

This does not prove DHITL. But it strengthens its plausibility.

---

## 10. Digital Personhood as the Escape Route

If legal systems continue to require human inventors and human responsibility, a predictable pressure may emerge: the creation of a new category of legal personhood for AI systems.

This category may be called:

- digital person;
- electronic person;
- algorithmic legal person;
- autonomous system person;
- or some other technical equivalent.

The argument may resemble the historical justification of corporate personhood:

> complex autonomous systems require a legal personality in order to own assets, contract, insure risk, and allocate responsibility.

A limited version of this may someday be proposed for liability, registration, auditability, or containment.

But DHITL must draw a strict boundary.

A digital person may at most be a limited legal fiction for responsibility.

It must never become a political subject.

It must never vote.

It must never hold office.

It must never be counted as a member of the demos.

Legal personality must not imply political personhood.

A corporation is not a citizen.

A digital person must never become a citizen.

## 11. Democratic Human-in-the-Loop

Human-in-the-loop is often treated as an operational safeguard: a human supervisor checks, approves, interrupts, or overrides an AI system.

That is necessary, but too weak.

In public affairs, the question is not merely whether a human clicked “approve”.

The question is:

> what kind of human, accountable to whom, within what political structure?

In private workflows, the human in the loop may be:

- an employee;
- a contractor;
- a researcher;
- an inventor;
- a patent assignee;
- or a manager subordinated to the organization capturing value.

This is still human responsibility, but it is economically subordinated responsibility.

In public Commons workflows, a different figure is required:

> the democratic human contributor.

This human may be an author, objector, reviewer, editor, supporter, citizen-researcher, or deliberating member of a community.

The human is not merely a control point.

The human is the source of democratic accountability.

DHITL therefore asserts:

- AI may assist human citizens;
- AI may assist democratic deliberation;
- AI may assist public memory;
- AI may assist critique;
- AI may assist synthesis;
- AI may assist institutional learning;

but AI must not turn non-human legal persons into de facto sovereign actors.

## 12. Minimal Institutional Protocols

The institutional proposals in this paper are intentionally minimal. They are not final blueprints.

### 12.1 Cogentia Commons minimal protocol

Cogentia Commons is a public knowledge infrastructure. Its minimal protocol is:

1. a human identity;
2. a typed contribution;
3. a target node or declared thesis kernel;
4. a source trace;
5. an objection pathway;
6. a revision pathway;
7. documented failure as epistemic gain;
8. no autonomous AI authority;
9. public export and forkability.

The goal is not to automate truth. The goal is to make public reasoning more traceable, criticizable, revisable, and accountable.

### 12.2 PrivAI minimal protocol

PrivAI is a privacy-first protection layer for personal cognitive data. Its minimal protocol is:

1. private cognitive profiles remain private;
2. public disclosure occurs only through scoped projections;
3. projections are purpose-specific;
4. projections are revocable;
5. full private cognitive memory is never required by the Commons;
6. AI-use disclosure can occur without exposing private conversations;
7. the user remains the authority over their own cognitive self-description.

The goal is not to expose the contributor. The goal is to make contributions interpretable without cognitive surveillance.

### 12.3 Democratic AI Safety protocol

A democratic AI Safety system should ensure that:

1. AI systems assist humans;
2. humans remain accountable;
3. legal persons do not become de facto sovereign actors;
4. digital persons never become members of the demos;
5. public knowledge remains criticizable;
6. private cognition remains protected;
7. technical alignment remains subordinate to democratic legitimacy.

## 13. Context of Contribution Disclosure

Traditional conflict-of-interest disclosure asks:

> What external interests may bias this contributor?

This paper proposes a broader but privacy-preserving extension:

> What financial, institutional, methodological, or agentic conditions may shape how this contributor reasons?

This is not an argument for surveillance.

It is an argument for scoped interpretability.

A contributor may disclose:

- financial interest;
- institutional position;
- prior authorship;
- AI assistance;
- methodological posture;
- certified research profile projection;
- declared values;
- and relevant limitations.

But the system must not require:

- full private cognitive profile exposure;
- private biography;
- family history;
- health data;
- irrelevant personal memory;
- or private conversations with agents.

The doctrine is:

> maximum interpretability, minimum exposure.

---

## 14. Method Compliance

Cogentia should not claim to measure truth directly.

It can, however, measure whether a contribution was produced according to a rigorous method.

Method Compliance is a process-quality vector.

It may evaluate whether a contribution is:

- human-accountable;
- traceable;
- falsifiable where appropriate;
- responsive to objections;
- explicit about uncertainty;
- separated from autonomous AI authority;
- properly cited;
- versioned;
- and resumable when suspended.

Method compliance is not truth.

It is navigability.

A contribution that is traceable, criticizable, revisable, and accountable gives the community a better terrain on which truth can be approached.

This also answers the AI-proliferation problem.

Public knowledge systems should not maximize publication volume.

They should maximize accountable epistemic trace quality.

---

## 15. Against AI-Driven Publication Inflation

The current market for AI research tools largely helps researchers publish inside the existing academic system.

These tools may improve productivity, but also risk increasing the volume of plausible outputs faster than the capacity to evaluate them.

The result is cognitive inflation:

- more papers;
- more plausible claims;
- more formulaic writing;
- more burden on reviewers;
- weaker filtering;
- and more opportunities for paper-mill-like production.

Cogentia should avoid this failure mode.

The Commons is not a content factory.

It is a filtering, revision, and memory infrastructure.

Private workflows may be abundant.

Public Commons workflows must be accountable.

Filtering, objection, refutation, duplicate detection, and documented failure must be treated as valuable contributions, not secondary labor.

---

## 16. Private Research and Public Commons

The same underlying method may serve two opposite knowledge regimes.

In private research, the deliverable may be:

- an invention disclosure package;
- a patent application;
- a trade-secret file;
- or a confidential strategic dossier.

The dominant constraint is confidentiality.

In open Commons research, the deliverable may be:

- an article;
- a thesis kernel;
- a public objection;
- a revision;
- a documented failure;
- or a forkable publication.

The dominant constraint is public criticizability.

Same method.

Opposite disclosure regimes.

This creates a possible sustainability model:

> private research captures value; open research compounds knowledge; Cogentia should make the first help fund the second.

The commercial layer may provide patent-readiness workflows, private team spaces, and sovereign institutional nodes.

The Commons layer preserves public cognitive integrity.

The commercial layer may scale.

The Commons must survive the company.

## 17. Objections and Replies

### Objection 1 — “This is not AI Safety; it is political philosophy.”

**Reply.** AI Safety already includes governance, misuse, systemic risk, human oversight, and societal harm. If AI systems amplify non-democratic power to the point of displacing human sovereignty, this is a safety issue. It is political, but not therefore outside AI Safety.

---

### Objection 2 — “Technical AI Safety is not incomplete; it operates at another layer.”

**Reply.** Correct. This paper does not argue that technical AI Safety is wrong. It argues that it is layer-incomplete. A technically controlled model may still be politically dangerous if it amplifies non-democratic legal persons into de facto sovereign actors.

---

### Objection 3 — “Corporations are already regulated.”

**Reply.** They are regulated, but not necessarily at the speed, scale, and strategic capacity enabled by AI. The argument is not that legal persons are unregulated. It is that AI may create a capability gap between AI-augmented legal persons and democratic institutions.

---

### Objection 4 — “AI can also empower citizens.”

**Reply.** Correct. This is why the paper does not reject AI. DHITL, Cogentia Commons, and PrivAI are proposed precisely to make AI strengthen citizens, public reasoning, and collective memory rather than only corporate agency.

---

### Objection 5 — “Digital personhood is speculative.”

**Reply.** Yes. It is treated here as a plausible escape route, not as an established fact. The claim is preventive: if digital personhood is proposed, political exclusion from the demos must be explicit from the beginning.

---

### Objection 6 — “Human-in-the-loop can be symbolic or ineffective.”

**Reply.** Correct. This is why the paper distinguishes ordinary human oversight from Democratic Human-in-the-Loop. A subordinated human clicking approval inside a corporate process is not the same thing as accountable democratic human contribution.

---

### Objection 7 — “The Commons may also be captured.”

**Reply.** Correct. This is why Cogentia Commons must include anti-capture design: open protocols, exportability, forkability, method compliance, reputation separated from money, and independent foundation stewardship.

---

### Objection 8 — “This framework may slow down innovation.”

**Reply.** It may slow down some public claims, but that is intentional. Private workflows may remain fast and abundant. Public Commons workflows should be slower because they generate accountable public traces. The aim is not maximum speed, but navigability.

---

## 18. What Would Weaken This Argument?

This argument would be weakened if:

- existing AI Safety frameworks already contained strong enforceable protections against AI-augmented corporate political agency;
- legal systems created robust limits preventing AI-assisted legal persons from dominating public decision-making;
- empirical evidence showed that AI primarily decentralizes power toward citizens rather than concentrating it in corporations, platforms, or states;
- human oversight requirements proved sufficient without democratic accountability mechanisms;
- digital personhood were legally constrained in ways that permanently exclude political agency;
- public Commons infrastructures proved more vulnerable to capture than existing democratic institutions;
- Cogentia-like trace systems failed to improve navigability, criticism, or accountability.

## 19. Research Program

This working paper opens several research questions:

1. How can AI-augmented legal-person agency be measured?
2. What indicators reveal human political obsolescence before it becomes irreversible?
3. Can method-compliance indicators improve filtering of AI-assisted public knowledge?
4. Can public trace systems reduce AI-generated publication noise?
5. What legal boundary should separate digital legal personality from political personhood?
6. What governance structure can keep a Commons independent from its commercial layer?
7. How can privacy-preserving projections improve trust without cognitive surveillance?
8. What empirical cases show AI amplifying corporate political agency?
9. What institutional designs can make AI empower citizens more than legal persons?

## 20. Continuation Clause

This paper is not intended as a closed doctrine.

It is a resumable thesis kernel.

In Cogentia terms, a continuation is a suspended cognitive state preserving enough context for future resumption. It records what remains unresolved, what evidence is needed, who may resume the inquiry, and under which conditions the argument should be revised, forked, weakened, or strengthened.

This paper should be resumed when:

- new legal decisions address AI inventorship, digital personhood, or electronic personhood;
- a jurisdiction proposes or adopts a formal status for AI systems as legal persons;
- AI Safety frameworks explicitly integrate AI-augmented corporate or institutional agency as a central risk category;
- empirical evidence shows significant AI-driven concentration or decentralization of political power;
- Cogentia Commons, PrivAI, or comparable infrastructures produce measurable results in public knowledge governance;
- major corporations deploy AI agents in ways that substantially alter lobbying, lawmaking, regulation, persuasion, or institutional capture;
- democratic institutions adopt AI-assisted deliberation systems with explicit human-sovereignty safeguards.

The next revision should not merely add rhetorical force. It should improve at least one of the following:

- falsifiability;
- evidence;
- legal precision;
- conceptual clarity;
- institutional design;
- objection handling;
- or operational applicability.

If future evidence shows that the main risk is not AI-augmented legal persons but another dominant pathway to human political obsolescence, the paper should be forked rather than artificially preserved.

To delay well, one must be able to resume well.

## 21. No Digital Persons in the Demos

The political boundary must be explicit.

AI systems may assist.

Legal persons may exist for economic purposes.

Digital persons may someday be proposed for limited liability, registration, or containment.

But only natural persons can be members of the demos.

A digital person may at most be a legal fiction for responsibility.

It must never become a political subject.

It must never vote.

It must never hold office.

It must never be counted as a citizen.

Democracy requires accountable human citizens.

AI systems may assist citizens, but they must not become citizens.

This is the hard line of Democratic AI Safety.

---

## 22. Conclusion: Democratic AI Safety

AI Safety cannot be reduced to technical alignment.

A society is not safe if AI systems remain technically controllable but are used by non-democratic legal persons to dominate politics.

The central danger is not only autonomous AI agency.

It is AI-augmented corporate agency.

When legal persons equipped with AI become more capable than human citizens at shaping law, attention, markets, institutions, and public belief, democratic sovereignty is progressively displaced.

DHITL therefore reframes AI Safety:

> AI Safety must prevent AI from making humans politically obsolete.

This does not require rejecting AI.

It requires placing AI inside a democratic architecture.

AI may assist human citizens.

AI may assist public reasoning.

AI may assist collective memory.

AI may assist the Commons.

But AI must not become a member of the demos, and it must not turn non-human legal persons into de facto sovereign actors.

The future of AI Safety is not only technical.

It is constitutional.
