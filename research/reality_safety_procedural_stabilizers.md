## Object and associated documents

This document proposes **Reality Safety** as a necessary enlargement of the frame we presently call **AI Safety**. Its central thesis is deliberately strong, and easily weaponized if it is handled badly: AI Safety is not the whole problem but the revealer of a deeper one, which may be called Reality Safety.

The danger of our moment is not only that artificial intelligence may produce outputs that are false, dangerous, biased, manipulative, addictive, opaque or beyond control. It is that artificial intelligence, by multiplying plausible appearances at industrial speed, lays bare the fragility of the procedures through which a society stabilizes reality into a common world — a world that must remain contestable, corrigible, plural, accountable and inhabitable. Once those conditions begin to deteriorate, the question is no longer merely whether this or that content happens to be true. It becomes something more disturbing: do we still possess the shared procedures by which truth, error, responsibility, fiction, hypothesis, memory and correction can be told apart?

This text should be treated as a **source document** within the corpus. It is not yet the public essay, the academic article, the political note, the Substack version, the LinkedIn note or the Facebook post; those later forms are to be derived from this source, and should not replace it. The source is the substance-bearing layer; the derived forms are scenes of appearance.

## Abstract

The current debate on AI Safety identifies, and rightly, the major risks attached to general-purpose AI systems: malicious use, technical failure, systemic risk, synthetic media, dependence on a small number of providers, loss of oversight, cyber and biological misuse, the disruption of labour, institutional fragility, and the concentration of cognitive infrastructure in a few hands. These concerns are serious, and none of them should be dismissed.

Yet they do not exhaust the problem. AI Safety is also a sign — and perhaps, today, the most visible revealer — of a crisis deeper and older than itself: the crisis of **Reality Safety**.

Reality Safety is the capacity of a society to maintain a shared reality that remains traceable, contestable, corrigible, plural, humanly accountable and institutionally inhabitable — and to maintain it under conditions of complexity, mediation, synthetic content, platform incentives, agentic delegation and institutional distrust.

The particular contribution of this document is not to add yet another label to the existing work on information integrity, epistemic security, social epistemology, fact-checking or media provenance. It is to extend provenance from **media objects** to **claims, derivations, reasoning chains, transformations, personas, mandates, publication acts and correction pathways**. A content credential may tell us where an image came from; Reality Safety asks the broader, procedural question: from which source, through which transformation, under whose responsibility, with which objections, toward which public form, and by what path of correction did this appearance enter the shared world?

Artificial intelligence does not merely generate false content. It increases the number, the speed and the plausibility of appearances whose source, status, mandate, authorship, transformation history and responsibility remain unclear. In doing so it intensifies what the earlier Ubik document called the **Ubik effect**: the subjective and collective experience of reality dislocation that sets in when signs, institutions, messages, objects and personas lose two stabilizing conditions at once — **provenance**, the capacity to return to a stable source, and **operational consistency**, the capacity to rely on objects, messages and institutions as persistent enough to act upon.

The answer cannot be a magical stabilizer — one central authority, one platform, one fact-checker, one oracle model, one identity layer, one watermark, one ministry, one official narrative. Stabilizers of that kind may quiet anxiety, but they risk becoming monopolies of reality. Nor should procedural stabilization pretend that it escapes capture by its nature. Its justification is at once more modest and stronger: when it is properly designed, its failure modes remain more visible, more contestable and more correctable than those of the magical, authoritarian, market or cynical stabilizers.

Reality Safety therefore reframes AI Safety as one layer within a larger task: that of preserving the conditions under which reality can still be sourced, reasoned about, tested, corrected, shared and inhabited.

This source document follows the discipline of the **second method**: it treats objections as first-class contributions, separates its levels of evidence, preserves the distinction between source and derived products, publishes the process of transformation wherever that is useful, and leaves the final arbitration to the human author. Its literary form is therefore not a relaxation of method; it is a deliberate rhetorical form set within a traceable corpus architecture.

## Core commitments of this source document

This draft rests on seven commitments that should survive every derived version:

1. **AI Safety is necessary but insufficient.** It reveals a broader crisis of shared-world maintenance.
2. **Reality Safety is procedural, not authoritarian.** It is not a ministry of truth, a truth oracle, or a monopoly of verification.
3. **The operational novelty is claim and reasoning provenance.** Reality Safety extends provenance beyond media objects toward claims, derivations, reasoning chains, personas, mandates, publication acts and correction pathways.
4. **The Ubik effect is the diagnostic background.** The problem is not merely falsehood, but the simultaneous weakening of provenance and operational consistency: one no longer knows reliably where an appearance comes from, nor whether it remains stable enough to guide action.
5. **Source, form and platform must remain distinct.** A public text, a platform post, a summary, a screenshot or an AI answer must not silently replace its source.
6. **Non-naïve seriousness must be preserved, but as a bounded Level-B implication.** A society needs the capacity to affirm provisionally, traceably and corrigibly, without falling into either credulity or cynicism; however, the psychological and sociological claims attached to nihilism, boreout and meaning-loss are not the critical path of this source document.
7. **Procedural stabilizers do not escape capture by essence.** They are justified because their failures can be made more visible, contestable and correctable than the failures of magical, authoritarian, market or cynical stabilizers.

## Claims registry

This section records the main claims of the document in a form that remains readable by a human reader while also being explicit enough for later extraction by Cogentia, review agents, derived products or implementation work.

A claim is not yet a proof. It is a stabilized proposition that can be sourced, challenged, refined, transformed, implemented or rejected. The purpose of this registry is not to interrupt the literary flow of the essay, but to make its argumentative structure inspectable.

### Claim RS-01 — AI Safety is necessary but insufficient

**Claim.** AI Safety is necessary, but it does not exhaust the problem raised by artificial intelligence. AI systems may be dangerous in themselves, but they also reveal a deeper fragility: the weakness of the procedures by which a society maintains a shared, corrigible and accountable reality.

**Evidence level.** Level B — defensible interpretation.

**Rationale.** AI Safety addresses model behaviour, misuse, deployment risk, transparency, systemic risk and technical robustness. Reality Safety asks what remains when the output of AI systems enters public reality: who is responsible, what is the source, what reasoning led to the claim, what transformation occurred, and how correction remains possible.

**Risk of overreach.** The claim must not imply that AI caused the entire crisis. The safer formulation is that AI reveals, accelerates and intensifies a pre-existing crisis.

**Testable form.** If AI systems became technically safer — better disclosure, fewer hallucinations, stronger watermarking, auditable interfaces — and yet reality dislocation persisted because claims, summaries, personas and institutional acts remained detached from source, reasoning, responsibility and correction, then the residual problem would be Reality Safety.

### Claim RS-02 — Reality Safety is procedural, not substantive truth control

**Claim.** Reality Safety is not a ministry of truth, an oracle, a platform trust score, a censorship regime or a centralized authority over reality. It is a procedural framework for preserving the conditions under which claims and appearances can be sourced, traced, contested, corrected and responsibly published.

**Evidence level.** Definitional / normative.

**Rationale.** A plural society cannot and should not impose one final reality from above. It must instead preserve shared procedures through which disagreement remains livable and correction remains possible.

**Risk of misreading.** The term “Reality Safety” is strong and may be interpreted as a call for control over truth. Every public derivative should therefore subtitle it procedurally: claim provenance, reasoning provenance and procedural stabilizers for shared reality.

### Claim RS-03 — The operational novelty is claim and reasoning provenance

**Claim.** The specific contribution of Reality Safety is to extend provenance beyond media objects toward claims, derivations, reasoning chains, transformations, mandates, personas, publication acts and correction pathways.

**Evidence level.** Level B / Level C — defensible extension and corpus-specific formulation.

**Rationale.** Content provenance can tell us where an image, video or file came from. Reality Safety asks how a claim entered the shared world: from which source, through which transformation, under whose mandate, with what reasoning, with which objections, under whose responsibility, and by what correction path.

**Operational consequence.** A Reality Safety envelope should not merely record whether a text, image or output is AI-generated. It should record the relation between source, claim, reasoning, transformation, responsible person, platform appearance and return-to-corpus mechanism.

### Claim RS-04 — The Ubik effect is the diagnostic background

**Claim.** The Ubik effect designates the subjective and collective dislocation of reality that occurs when provenance and operational consistency degrade together.

**Evidence level.** Level C — corpus-specific diagnostic concept, grounded in literary analogy and contemporary examples.

**Rationale.** Reality does not disappear; it becomes procedurally harder to hold. A screenshot becomes a source. A generated image becomes evidence. A summary replaces a document. A persona appears as a responsible subject. A platform trend appears as public opinion.

**Two dimensions.** The Ubik effect involves both:

1. loss of provenance — the inability to return from appearance to source;
2. loss of operational consistency — the inability to rely on objects, messages, institutions or personas as stable enough to guide action.

**Function in the document.** The Ubik effect is diagnostic, not decorative. It explains why AI Safety must be widened into Reality Safety.

### Claim RS-05 — Source, form and platform must remain distinct

**Claim.** Reality dislocation increases when source, form and platform are confused.

**Evidence level.** Level B / methodological.

**Rationale.** The source is the substance-bearing, versioned, corrigible corpus. The form is the derived product adapted to an audience or use. The platform is the scene of appearance, circulation and optimization. When a platform post replaces the source, or when a public essay replaces the corpus, or when a summary replaces the document, memory and responsibility drift.

**Operational consequence.** Every derived product should preserve a return path to the source and should record its transformation from the source.

### Claim RS-06 — Procedural stabilizers are justified by correctability, not purity

**Claim.** Procedural stabilizers do not escape capture by essence. They are justified only insofar as their failures remain more visible, contestable and correctable than those of magical, authoritarian, market or cynical stabilizers.

**Evidence level.** Level B / normative design principle.

**Rationale.** A procedure can become bureaucratic, elitist, slow, captured or formalistic. The answer is not to pretend procedure is pure, but to design it so that its failures can be inspected, objected to, versioned, corrected and locally bypassed when necessary.

**Anti-capture rule.** Anti-capture is not a status. It is a discipline.

### Claim RS-07 — False stabilizers respond to the same dislocation but fail dangerously

**Claim.** Magical, authoritarian, market and cynical stabilizers are understandable responses to reality dislocation, but each tends toward a dangerous form of capture.

**Evidence level.** Level B — interpretive typology.

**Rationale.** Magical stabilizers promise that one tool or oracle will restore reality. Authoritarian stabilizers impose reality from above. Market stabilizers sell trust as dependency. Cynical stabilizers dissolve the need for correction by declaring that nothing holds.

**Design implication.** Reality Safety must answer the same need for stabilization without becoming a monopoly of reality.

### Claim RS-08 — Non-naïve seriousness is a necessary but bounded implication

**Claim.** Reality Safety requires a form of non-naïve seriousness: the capacity to affirm provisionally, traceably and corrigibly, without falling into either credulity or generalized cynicism.

**Evidence level.** Level B — philosophical and sociological implication, not critical path.

**Rationale.** If every claim is treated as manipulation, posture or spectacle, correction becomes pointless. But if claims are accepted without source or trace, credulity returns. Non-naïve seriousness is the middle discipline: source, trace, object, correct, assume responsibility.

**Boundary.** Claims about nihilism, boreout, meaning-loss and psychological fatigue should remain outside the critical path unless separately grounded with stronger evidence.

### Claim RS-09 — Reality Safety is democratic only if it remains plural and subsidiarity-based

**Claim.** Reality Safety becomes democratic only when it preserves pluralism, locality, objection, correction and anti-monopoly governance.

**Evidence level.** Level B / normative-political.

**Rationale.** A society should not need one central actor to certify reality. It should possess distributed procedures by which citizens, institutions, associations, researchers, journalists and local communities can preserve sources, register objections, correct errors and maintain public memory.

**Connection.** This claim links Reality Safety to the broader doctrine of Autonomy of Capacity: a capable society is not one that is told what reality is, but one that can maintain, contest and correct its own reality.

### Claim RS-10 — Reality Safety must distinguish verifiable, socially attested and declarative fields

**Claim.** A procedural stabilizer must not confuse what is cryptographically verifiable, what is socially attested and what is merely declarative.

**Evidence level.** Level B / technical-design principle.

**Rationale.** A checksum is not the same kind of evidence as a human review declaration. A signed commit is not the same kind of evidence as a stated audience. If a stabilizer mixes these categories, it recreates the Ubik effect inside the stabilizer itself.

**Operational classes.**

- **V** — cryptographically or computationally verifiable;
- **S** — socially or institutionally attested;
- **D** — declarative-only metadata.

### Claim RS-11 — Fractanet should treat Reality Safety as a native property of packets, traces and mandates

**Claim.** If Fractanet becomes an AI-first distributed infrastructure, Reality Safety should not be added later as an ethical overlay. It should be native to packets, traces, mandates, ledgers, correction channels and escalation rules.

**Evidence level.** Level C — corpus-specific infrastructure hypothesis.

**Rationale.** A Fractanet packet should carry enough procedural reality to remain accountable: source references, mandate, responsible person or institution, transformation trace, privacy level, objection hooks, correction channel, expiration policy and escalation rule.

**Boundary.** This source document establishes the requirement; a separate Fractanet design note should define the implementation.


### Claim RS-12 — Capability regimes operationalize Reality Safety under constraint

**Claim.** Reality Safety becomes operationally sharper when decisions are understood as regime-relative: a claim, action, packet or mandate is not evaluated in the abstract, but under constraints of time, uncertainty, stakes, reversibility, resources, mandate, fallback and traceability.

**Evidence level.** Level C — Fractanet bridge hypothesis.

**Rationale.** The same appearance or decision may require different procedures depending on whether the regime is normal, perturbed, degraded, critical, vital or recovery. A casual AI summary, a public accusation, a legal claim, a medical instruction and an emergency infrastructure decision should not carry the same trace, review or responsibility requirements.

**Operational consequence.** Fractanet capability regimes should reinforce Reality Safety by making the decision context explicit: what was known, what was uncertain, what deadline applied, what capacities were mobilized, who or what had the mandate to act, what fallback existed, and what trace was preserved.

**Boundary.** This document does not define the full regime protocol. That belongs in `FractaVolta/research/capability_regimes.md`.

## Academic grounding note

This version adds a targeted pass of academic grounding. Its aim is not to decorate the thesis with famous names, but to attach each fragile claim to a relevant body of work, and to mark the boundary between what is established knowledge, what is defensible interpretation, what is corpus hypothesis and what is mere analogy.

The central thesis remains original in its synthesis: **Reality Safety** is proposed here as a procedural enlargement of AI Safety. Its components, however, are not isolated inventions. They can be situated within at least nine established conversations:

1. **AI Safety and AI governance**, which document risks from general-purpose AI systems, malicious use, technical failure and systemic integration.
2. **Provenance and synthetic-content transparency**, including C2PA, Content Credentials, EU AI Act Article 50 and their technical/legal limitations.
3. **Information disorder and truth decay**, which already describe the degradation of public informational environments beyond the narrow term “fake news”.
4. **Social epistemology**, which studies knowledge as socially produced, trusted, criticized, transmitted and institutionally mediated.
5. **Shared reality and common ground**, which show that communication and social coordination depend on procedures of mutual understanding.
6. **Boredom, boreout and meaning-loss**, which support a cautious reading of under-stimulation, lack of challenge and crisis of meaning as real psychological and organizational phenomena.
7. **Cynicism and political disengagement**, which support the distinction between productive skepticism and destructive cynicism.
8. **Anomie, acceleration and modern malaise**, which provide a sociological and philosophical background for disorientation under rapid change.
9. **Cybernetics, homeostasis and procedural regulation**, which provide the broader frame for treating stabilization as a dynamic process rather than a fixed truth-state.

This grounding also clarifies the comparative position of the concept. Reality Safety is not offered as a synonym for information integrity or for epistemic security. It is proposed as a procedural extension of them: from object provenance to claim provenance; from content authenticity to reasoning accountability; from media disclosure to the discipline of source, form and platform; from fact-checking after the fact of circulation to a corrigibility built into publication and delegation themselves.

None of this proves the full Reality Safety thesis. It strengthens the evidential status of its components, and clarifies what still remains a corpus hypothesis.

## 1. Central thesis

One may begin with a statement simple enough to be suspect:

> AI Safety is a revealer of Reality Safety.

By this I do not mean that AI Safety is false, useless or secondary. It is necessary. Artificial intelligence systems can cause concrete harm: they can deceive, they can amplify manipulation, generate plausible falsehoods, assist cyberattacks, lower the barriers to dangerous knowledge, automate fragile decisions, produce dependency and concentrate power. These dangers are real.

But a danger may also possess a diagnostic value. It may show us that the fragile object is not only the tool, but the world into which the tool is introduced. Artificial intelligence appears within societies already marked by distrust, by platform fragmentation, by institutional fatigue, by an excess of information, and by a growing difficulty in telling source from form, form from platform, persona from subject, summary from document, and appearance from responsibility. It accelerates these confusions; it did not create all of them.

The deeper danger, then, is not only that people may come to believe false things. It is that they may lose their confidence in the very procedures by which truth, error, correction, responsibility and shared meaning are distinguished. When that happens, the problem ceases to be merely epistemic: it becomes institutional, psychological and political at once.

The distinctive move of Reality Safety is to place **claim and reasoning provenance** at the centre. Existing provenance work can establish the origin and the editing history of a digital object; Reality Safety asks how a claim, a summary, a recommendation, an accusation, a public statement, a delegated act or an AI-assisted publication may be traced back to its source, its reasoning, its mandate, its transformation, its responsible person and its channel of correction. This is the step that carries the concept beyond a merely useful re-bundling of older concerns.

The question is not only:

```text
How do we make AI systems safe?
```

It is also:

```text
How do we keep reality socially maintainable when AI multiplies appearances faster than institutions, communities and persons can stabilize their provenance, reasoning and responsibility?
```

This is the Reality Safety problem.

## 2. Why AI Safety is necessary but insufficient

The frame of AI Safety remains indispensable, for artificial intelligence systems can produce real harms. They may be put to malicious use; they may scale deception; they may serve cyber or biological misuse; they may automate harmful decisions; they may give rise to unsafe delegation; they may hallucinate; they may produce synthetic media that erodes trust; they may discriminate; they may manipulate; they may wear away human oversight; and they may concentrate power in the hands of a few providers.

No serious doctrine should make light of these risks. A system that can speak, classify, plan, imitate, infer and act through tools is no ordinary artifact; and once it is connected to institutions, platforms, markets, archives and the instruments of persuasion, its errors are no longer isolated errors.

And yet AI Safety remains insufficient so long as it confines itself to model behaviour, deployment risk, user harm, misuse, the control of capability, content moderation or technical robustness. These objects matter, but they remain too close to the machine. The larger question concerns the environment in which humans, institutions, agents, platforms, documents and synthetic media meet and interact. Does that environment still allow reality to remain sourceable, traceable, contestable, corrigible, accountable, plural and inhabitable?

A model that is technically safer may still contribute to reality dislocation: if it produces plausible appearances without adequate provenance, if its outputs are cut off from their source corpora, if its personas obscure responsibility, if its summaries quietly replace the documents they summarize, or if its sheer speed overwhelms the procedures of correction.

The revealer claim can be put in a falsifiable form. Suppose that AI systems became technically safer — no hallucination in ordinary use, robust disclosure, strong watermarking, clear model cards, controlled deployment, auditable interfaces. A residual crisis would remain if claims, summaries, platform forms, institutional acts and delegated decisions still lacked their source, their reasoning provenance, their responsibility and their pathways of correction. That residual is the Reality Safety problem.

The point is not that model safety is irrelevant. It is that model safety becomes socially meaningful only within a larger architecture of reality maintenance.

## 3. Definition of Reality Safety

**Reality Safety** is the set of technical, institutional, cognitive and social conditions that allow a community to maintain a shared reality — one that remains traceable, contestable, corrigible, plural and accountable — under conditions of complexity, mediation, synthetic content and agentic delegation.

The definition is designed to avoid two opposite errors. On the one hand it does not reduce the question to fact-checking; on the other it does not erect a sovereign authority charged with declaring what is real. Reality Safety is not an official ministry of truth, nor a single fact-checking authority, nor a central model that determines truth, nor a platform trust score, nor a universal identity regime, nor a censorship apparatus, nor a rejection of pluralism, nor a nostalgic return to pre-digital authority.

It is, instead, procedural. It asks along which paths claims and appearances can be sourced, located, versioned, contextualized, attributed, challenged, corrected, retracted, reinstated, derived, published, audited, remembered and — when there is reason for it — forgotten.

The core issue is not to abolish uncertainty, which would be at once impossible and politically dangerous. The core issue is to make uncertainty workable.

A society has no need of immediate agreement upon every fact, interpretation or value. What it needs are procedures shared widely enough that disagreement does not become uninhabitable.

## 4. The Ubik effect as diagnostic background

The earlier Ubik document defined the **Ubik effect** as the subjective and collective experience of reality dislocation that is produced when the signs, objects, institutions and messages which once stabilized a shared world begin to regress, to contradict one another, to mutate, or to lose their provenance. It was not simply a theory of illusion; it was a theory of the destabilized conditions of appearance.

For the present document, the concept must be made more operational. The Ubik effect designates the degradation of two stabilizing conditions at once.

First, there is a degradation of **provenance**: the capacity to return from an appearance to a stable source, a version, an author, a mandate, a transformation history and a responsible subject. A thing appears, speaks, circulates or persuades, but the path back to its source becomes uncertain.

Second, there is a degradation of **operational consistency**: the capacity to rely on objects, messages, institutions and identities as persistent enough across time to guide action. A document changes status without trace; a public statement survives only as a screenshot; a platform form replaces the institutional act; a persona behaves like a subject without being one; a generated image is treated as evidence until its status mutates under scrutiny.

The Ubik effect begins when these two degradations reinforce each other. Appearances remain abundant, often more abundant than before, but the procedures that would allow one to relate an appearance to a source, a transformation, a mandate, a responsible subject and a pathway of correction grow too weak to stabilize a common world.

Reality Safety is the policy, technical and methodological response to that diagnosis. The Ubik effect names the condition; Reality Safety names the problem-domain; the procedural stabilizers name the response.

```text
Ubik effect
→ loss of provenance + loss of operational consistency
→ reality dislocation

Reality Safety
→ preservation of shared reality under dislocation pressure

Procedural stabilizers
→ concrete mechanisms that keep reality traceable, corrigible and accountable
```

The literary importance of *Ubik* lies precisely here: the world does not simply become false. It becomes unreliable in its very modes of persistence. Objects regress; signs alter; messages arrive from uncertain ontological levels; what had seemed stable proves negotiable, decaying, mediated or manipulated. A world of this kind is terrifying not because some single statement is false, but because the procedures by which one would ordinarily re-establish what is happening no longer suffice.

This literary reference must not become mere atmosphere. Its function here is diagnostic. It allows us to name a practical failure that contemporary technical societies increasingly encounter: the abundance of convincing appearances without sufficient procedural means to stabilize their source, status, transformation and responsibility.

A modern example is simple. An AI-generated image circulates as though it were a journalistic photograph. Later, someone adds a label, someone else removes context, a platform crops the image, a commentator screenshots it, and the screenshot is then used as evidence in a new discussion. The issue is not only whether the first image was “fake”. The issue is that each layer weakens the path back to source and changes the operational status of the object. What is it now: evidence, illustration, manipulation, satire, platform artifact, or political weapon?

The same structure appears when a summary replaces the document it summarized. The summary may be accurate, useful and honest; yet if it circulates without a return path to the source, without a transformation map, without review status and without a correction channel, it begins to act as a surrogate reality. The source has not been denied. It has simply become procedurally unreachable.

Modern technical societies therefore have their own versions of the Ubik dislocation. A screenshot circulates as though it were a source. A generated image presents itself as though it were a record. A summary takes the place of a document. A persona speaks as though it were a subject. A platform trend appears as though it were public opinion. A model returns a confident answer with no responsible memory behind it. The world has not vanished; it has merely become, procedurally, harder to hold.

## 5. From fake news to reality maintenance

The common frame is too narrow:

```text
false information
→ fact-checking
```

A better frame is:

```text
reality dislocation
→ reality maintenance
```

Fake news is but one manifestation of the problem. Reality dislocation embraces many other phenomena besides: screenshots mistaken for sources, summaries for documents, platform posts for doctrine, AI outputs for grounded knowledge, personas for subjects, synthetic images for evidence, edited images for raw records, generated intimacy for relation, institutional speech for living accountability, statistical confidence for truth, and cynicism for lucidity.

Reality Safety therefore shifts the question. It does not abandon the question:

```text
Is this claim true or false?
```

But it embeds it in a broader procedural inquiry:

```text
What is this appearance?
Where does it come from?
Who or what produced it?
Under what mandate?
From which source?
Through which transformations?
With what review?
With what responsibility?
How can it be corrected?
```

Truth remains essential. But truth becomes socially usable only by way of procedures that preserve its conditions of appearance, of contestation and of correction.

The difficulty of our period is that appearances grow ever cheaper, faster and more convincing, while the procedures of stabilization remain slow, fragile, local, underfunded, distrusted or captured. The imbalance is dangerous.

---

## 6. AI as accelerator of appearance

Artificial intelligence intensifies the Reality Safety problem because it industrializes plausible appearance itself.

It can generate plausible text without a grounded source, images without a camera, voices without bodies, faces without persons, expertise without responsibility, summaries without memory, arguments without commitment, intimacy without relation, agency without mortality, confidence without proof, personas without stable subjects, documents without a corpus, and continuity without lived experience.

The danger is not simply that such appearances are false. Some may be useful; some accurate; some fictional in legitimate ways; some artistically or pedagogically valuable; some operationally valid. The danger is that their **status** becomes unclear.

A generated image is not, in itself, a lie; but if it circulates without any stable indication of its origin, its transformation, its purpose and its responsibility, it helps to make a world in which images cease to carry their former evidentiary weight. A summary is not, in itself, a falsification; but if it supplants the source, and if the path back to that source is lost, it becomes a force of dislocation. An AI persona may be a useful interface; but if its relation to the living subject, the institution, the mandate or the agentic system behind it remains unclear, it becomes a mask whose accountability is unstable.

Reality Safety is therefore not an anti-AI doctrine. It is a doctrine against dislocation.

## 7. Stabilizing tendencies and the correctability test

When reality becomes expensive to maintain, individuals and societies alike go in search of stabilizers. This is only natural; the psyche, like the polity, cannot live indefinitely in pure uncertainty. But stabilizers do not all fail in the same manner. The right test is not whether a stabilizer is pure — none is pure — but whether its failure remains visible, contestable and correctable.

This is where the Ubik effect becomes politically dangerous. When provenance weakens, one no longer knows where appearances come from. When operational consistency weakens, one no longer knows whether they will remain stable enough to guide action. The result is not only error; it is fatigue. A tired society does not merely ask for truth. It asks for relief from the cost of orientation.

That relief can be sought in several directions. Each direction offers a way of making the world feel stable again. Each also carries its own capture risk.

The first tendency is the **magical stabilizer**. It promises that a single tool, a single model, a single label, a single certification, a single watermark or a single platform can restore trust. Install this layer, it says, and reality will hold once more. The fantasy is attractive because it converts a civilizational problem into a solution one can purchase or deploy. But no single artifact can stabilize reality for a plural society.

The second tendency is the **authoritarian stabilizer**. It imposes reality by taking control of archives, language, institutions, records, dissent and correction. It offers one reality, one archive, one authority, one permissible memory. It may indeed reduce visible dislocation, but only by destroying democratic correction; it is the dream of certainty, bought at the price of freedom.

The third tendency is the **market stabilizer**. It sells trust, verification, reputation, identity or safety as a proprietary service. Reality becomes a subscription; trust becomes dependency upon a platform; verification becomes a captive service. This does not abolish dislocation — it privatizes stabilization.

The fourth tendency is the **cynical stabilizer**. It declares that nothing holds at all — that everything is narrative, manipulation, interest, spectacle, posture. It wears the appearance of lucidity, since it refuses to be duped; but it is often nihilistic, for it protects the subject from naïveté only by disabling commitment, correction and responsibility.

The fifth tendency is the **procedural stabilizer** — the one this document defends, though it must never be taken for clean by nature. Procedures too can grow bureaucratic, elitist, slow, exclusionary, credentialist, platformized, or captured by institutions that confuse traceability with permission. A procedural stabilizer is not justified by being incapable of capture; it is justified only insofar as its capture remains the more correctable from within: through objections, versioning, plural ledgers, local alternatives, auditability, explicit responsibility, and the right to return to the source.

The asymmetry, then, is not one of purity against impurity. It is one of correctability against closure.

| Stabilizing tendency | Typical capture mode | Correctability problem |
|---|---|---|
| Magical | dependency on one artifact or oracle | failure is hidden behind belief in the artifact |
| Authoritarian | imposed archive, imposed language, imposed memory | correction becomes dissent against power |
| Market | proprietary trust and rented verification | correction depends on platform permission |
| Cynical | inner resignation and generalized suspicion | correction appears pointless before it begins |
| Procedural | bureaucracy, elitism, checklist formalism | failure can remain inspectable if the procedure itself is open, local, contestable and versioned |

Anti-capture, therefore, is not a category to which one belongs. It is a discipline one must keep on exercising.

## 8. The nihilistic temptation as a bounded Level-B implication

The nihilistic temptation is important; but it is not the critical path of this source document. The procedural thesis does not depend on establishing a full psychological or sociological theory of contemporary malaise. It rests first of all on provenance, on traceability, on correction, on responsibility, and on the discipline of source, form and platform.

What may nonetheless be said, and said with caution, is this: the Ubik effect plausibly produces fatigue. The claim is interpretive, and should remain at **Level B** until it is strengthened by specific references on trust, cynicism, institutional disorientation, boredom, boreout, meaning-loss and democratic disengagement.

The mechanism is not mysterious, but it must not be overstated. Loss of provenance increases the effort required to know where an appearance comes from. Loss of operational consistency increases the effort required to know whether that appearance can still be relied upon. When both conditions degrade together, orientation itself becomes costly. The subject must ask, repeatedly: what is this, where does it come from, who speaks, what has been transformed, what can still be trusted, and where can correction occur?

At that point irony grows attractive. It says: I am not fooled; I am above belief; I do not take the bait. It protects the subject against naïveté by refusing adhesion. In a world saturated with uncertain appearances, this refusal can feel like intelligence.

The academic grounding added in v0.4 does not establish a single causal chain running from AI to nihilism. It supports only the narrower claim that modern societies already face documented problems — information disorder, a diminished public reliance on facts, social acceleration, institutional distrust, workplace boredom, a crisis of meaning, political cynicism. AI may well intensify these pressures, by multiplying plausible appearances and by raising the cognitive cost of orientation. That is a plausible synthesis, not a settled diagnosis.

Used locally, irony is useful. It guards against idols, slogans, propaganda, false seriousness and sentimental manipulation; it can reopen a distance where adhesion has become dangerous.

Used globally, however, irony can turn into a masked cynicism. It says, though not always openly: I will not affirm; I will not construct; I will not be held accountable; I will reduce every claim to posture; I will treat sincerity itself as weakness. This is the cynical stabilizer in its subjective form: the world becomes stable again because nothing is allowed to matter.

This is why Reality Safety must defend a **non-naïve seriousness** — though the phrase must be made operational rather than left to moralize. It means the discipline of provisional affirmation under conditions of traceability and correction.

The procedural stabilizer does not say:

```text
Believe.
```

It says:

```text
Source.
Trace.
Object.
Correct.
Assume responsibility.
Keep the world corrigible.
```

A society that can no longer take anything seriously except its own irony becomes incapable of transmission. This last sentence is a philosophical warning and not an empirical conclusion. It belongs in the document because it names one possible existential consequence of reality dislocation; it must not be pressed into service as proof of the procedural thesis.

## 9. Procedural stabilizers

A procedural stabilizer is not a truth machine. It is a mechanism that preserves the conditions under which claims, appearances, identities and transformations can be evaluated at all.

The minimal stabilizers are the following:

| Stabilizer | Function |
|---|---|
| Source corpus | Preserves the substance-bearing layer |
| Versioning | Prevents silent replacement |
| Provenance | Shows where an object or claim comes from |
| Traceability | Records transformations and acts |
| Qualified objections | Convert feelings of doubt into contestable claims |
| Explicit personas | Distinguish subject, role, agent, mask and mandate |
| Publication ledger | Records what appeared, where, when and under which form |
| Return-to-corpus | Prevents platform drift from replacing source memory |
| Human responsibility | Ensures that living persons remain accountable |
| Correction without erasure | Allows repair without destroying history |
| Anti-capture governance | Prevents one actor from monopolizing stabilization |

The point of procedure is not the worship of bureaucracy. Procedure matters because plural societies cannot rest on substantive agreement alone. People will disagree about facts, about values, about interpretations and priorities. A democratic society does not require immediate agreement upon every truth; it requires shared procedures through which disagreement can remain livable, corrigible and accountable.

The stabilizer, then, is not the final answer. It is the path along which answers can be proposed, criticized, corrected, resumed and transmitted.

## 10. The source / form / platform distinction

A practical Reality Safety principle is:

```text
source ≠ form ≠ platform
```

The **source** is the substance-bearing, versioned, corrigible corpus layer. The **form** is the derived product, adapted to an audience, a style, a format or a use. The **platform** is the scene of appearance, of circulation, of optimization and of reception.

Reality dislocation increases whenever these layers are confused. A Facebook post is taken for doctrine; a screenshot for a source; a Substack essay for the corpus; an academic article for the sovereign source; an AI answer for grounded knowledge; a persona for the living subject.

The distinction is not merely editorial; it is civilizational in miniature. If the source disappears into the platform, memory comes to depend on circulation. If the form is mistaken for the source, rhetoric takes the place of substance. If the platform becomes sovereign, visibility takes the place of validity.

The distinction is also the operational bridge between Reality Safety and claim and reasoning provenance. To return from a platform appearance back to a source is not merely to retrieve a file; it is to recover the path by which a claim was selected, transformed, simplified, adapted, reviewed, published, corrected, or left unresolved. A stabilizer that records only the origin of a media object is useful, but incomplete. A stabilizer that records source, form, platform and transformation begins to render reasoning itself accountable.

This gives the minimal architecture:

| Layer | Question | Failure when confused |
|---|---|---|
| Source | What is the versioned substance-bearing object? | platform memory replaces corpus memory |
| Form | What transformation made this object public, useful or persuasive? | rhetoric is mistaken for doctrine |
| Platform | Where did this form appear and under which incentives? | visibility is mistaken for validity |
| Return path | How can one go back, object, correct or supersede? | publication becomes irreversible appearance |

The claim envelope of section 22 is the first small instrument of this distinction. Its purpose is not to certify truth, nor to pretend that implementation is already solved, but to keep the relation between source, form and platform open to inspection.

Reality Safety requires, above all, the ability to return from appearance to source.

## 11. Cogentia and Ubikia as procedural responses

Cogentia may be read as a procedural stabilizer of cognition. It preserves source documents, cognitive packets, continuations, objections, transformation maps, levels of evidence, self-evaluations, a machine-readable structure, the links between documents, and the distinctions between claim, hypothesis, analogy and rhetorical device.

Cogentia does not decide what is true. It preserves the conditions under which claims can be followed, challenged, resumed, corrected and transmitted.

Ubikia may be read as a procedural stabilizer of publication. It should preserve the distinction between source and derived product, the publication ledgers, the platform-specific transformations, the provenance of public forms, the review status, the correction pathways, the return-to-corpus mechanisms, and the option of delayed or delegated publication when manual drift becomes too costly.

Ubikia does not produce truth. It prevents publication from dislocating the corpus.

Fractanet carries the same concern outward, from documents to infrastructures. Its relevant primitives are traceable packets, fractal state, locality, ascending subsidiarity, RAIX redundancy, stigmergic coordination, procedural mandates and homeostatic regulation.

Capability regimes add the missing operational bridge: a packet, claim, mandate or action must be routed not only according to origin, destination and rights, but also according to the regime of constraint in which it acts — normal, perturbed, degraded, critical, vital or recovery. As uncertainty and stakes rise, the system should reinforce traceability, clarify fallback, limit preemption by mandate, and preserve responsibility.

A minimal mapping is already discernible. A Fractanet packet should carry more than payload and destination; it should carry enough procedural reality to remain accountable — source references, mandate, the responsible human or institution, a transformation trace, a privacy level, objection hooks, a correction channel, an expiration policy, and escalation rules. RAIX can then avoid any monopoly of stabilization, distributing publication ledgers, caches, verifications and traces across replaceable nodes; and stigmergic traces can render objections, stale claims, verified claims and unresolved contradictions visible without the need for a single central orchestrator. This document does not yet furnish the full technical schema; it establishes only the requirement that any such schema must satisfy.

The Reality Safety question for Fractanet becomes:

```text
How does an AI-first, distributed infrastructure preserve shared operational reality while remaining plural, local, resilient, corrigible and governable?
```

This question must be answered before such infrastructures grow powerful enough to answer it in our place.

## 12. Relation to AI regulation and provenance standards

The current regulatory and technical efforts point in the right direction, but they remain partial.

Legal frameworks increasingly require users to be informed when they interact with AI systems and require synthetic content to be marked or disclosed. This is important. But marking AI-generated content does not solve Reality Safety by itself. A label may say:

```text
AI-generated
```

It does not necessarily answer:

```text
generated from what?
under whose mandate?
with what transformations?
reviewed by whom?
published as what?
derived from which source?
correctable where?
responsible to whom?
```

Content provenance standards are useful in their turn. They can attach an origin and an edit history to digital content, and this is a necessary component of Reality Safety. But media provenance is not enough. Reality Safety also requires document provenance, claim provenance, reasoning provenance, persona provenance, institutional-mandate provenance, agentic-action provenance, publication provenance and correction provenance.

The contribution proposed here is therefore not “more provenance” in the abstract. It is a specific extension: from **object provenance** to **claim and reasoning provenance**.

| Provenance layer | Typical content-provenance answer | Reality Safety extension |
|---|---|---|
| Media origin | Where did this image, video or file come from? | What source corpus, document, testimony or act does this appearance derive from? |
| Edit history | What technical modifications were made? | What conceptual transformations, omissions, simplifications and rhetorical adaptations were made? |
| AI disclosure | Was AI used? | Which AI system assisted which step, under whose mandate, with what human review? |
| Claim provenance | Is the object authentic? | Which claims does this object make, omit, strengthen, weaken or introduce? |
| Reasoning provenance | Usually not addressed | Which derivation, inference, summary, analogy or judgment connects source to public claim? |
| Authorship | Who published or signed it? | Who is the living responsible person or accountable institution for correction? |
| Verification | Is the content authentic? | What objections remain, what claims are provisional, and where can correction occur? |
| Platform | Where is it displayed? | How does this platform form relate back to the source, the ledger and the corpus? |

A single example will suffice. A public, AI-assisted essay may contain a generated image bearing proper content credentials and still fail Reality Safety — if the claims it makes cannot be traced back to source documents, if the transformations from source to public form are hidden, if the responsible human is unclear, if the objections have vanished, or if there is no path of correction back to the corpus. Conversely, a modest Markdown document carrying a source commit, a transformation map, its objections, its review status and a correction channel may stabilize reality better than a polished but untraceable media object.

AI governance, as it currently stands, tends to address the transparency of synthetic content, the management of model risk, provider accountability, deepfake disclosure, technical robustness and watermarking. Reality Safety adds a further layer: the maintenance of a shared world, procedural trust, cognitive orientation, the discipline of source, form and platform, claim and reasoning provenance, non-naïve seriousness, and a democratic stabilization without monopoly.

## 13. Democratic Reality Safety

Reality Safety has a political dimension as well. The question is not only how individuals come to know what is true; it is how a democratic community preserves the very possibility of a common world without imposing a single official reality.

This requires a middle path between relativism and authoritarianism.

```text
relativism
= no shared correction

authoritarianism
= correction replaced by imposed reality

procedural democracy
= plural reality testing under shared rules
```

A democratic Reality Safety must therefore protect pluralism, objection and correction, access to sources, the integrity of archives, the memory of minorities, procedural fairness, human responsibility, non-monopolistic verification, and a subsidiarity reaching from the local to the global.

This connects directly with the broader doctrine of **Autonomy of Capacity**. A capable society is not one that is told what reality is; it is one that possesses the procedures, the tools and the institutions to maintain, contest and correct reality together.

The opposite of Reality Safety is not, in the end, misinformation. It is dependence — the condition in which a society must wait upon someone else to certify what it is permitted to perceive, to remember and to say.

## 14. Objections and responses

A source document must not shield itself from objections. It must invite them, qualify them, and keep them as working material.

### Objection 1 — “Reality Safety” sounds like a ministry of truth.

**Response.** That is precisely the danger the concept must avoid. Reality Safety is not a substantive control of truth; it is a procedural stabilization. It protects the conditions of correction rather than imposing a final reality. The term is retained, provisionally, because it names the target problem more forcefully than “information integrity” or “media provenance” — naming not merely the quality of information objects, but the safety of the shared procedures by which reality remains sourceable, corrigible and inhabitable.

**Evidence level:** definitional / normative.

### Objection 2 — AI Safety already covers this under systemic risk.

**Response.** In part. The discussions of AI Safety do take in systemic risk, disinformation, deepfakes, institutional impacts and dependency. But Reality Safety isolates the deeper structure beneath them: the maintenance of a shared and corrigible world. More precisely, it places at its centre claim and reasoning provenance, which cannot be reduced to model safety or to media authenticity.

**Evidence level:** interpretation of current governance frames.

### Objection 3 — The concept is too broad.

**Response.** It is broad because the problem is broad. The remedy is not to compress the concept prematurely, but to structure it into operational layers — provenance, versioning, traceability, objections, personas, publication ledgers, correction and governance. The operational core, by contrast, is deliberately narrow: to preserve the relation between source, claim, reasoning, transformation, responsibility, platform appearance and the path of correction.

**Evidence level:** methodological.

### Objection 4 — This overstates AI’s role.

**Response.** The thesis does not say that AI caused the crisis. It says that AI reveals it and intensifies it. The underlying crisis includes platform incentives, institutional distrust, the fragmentation of media, complexity, acceleration and nihilistic fatigue. The counterfactual test runs thus: if AI systems became technically safe and fully disclosed, and yet reality dislocation persisted because claims, summaries, personas, institutional acts and delegated decisions remained detached from source, reasoning, responsibility and correction, then the residual problem would be Reality Safety.

**Evidence level:** central distinction / falsifiable framing.

### Objection 5 — Procedural stabilizers may become bureaucratic or elitist.

**Response.** Yes; the risk is serious. Procedural stabilization is not clean in its essence. It can be captured by bureaucratic formalism, by an expert monopoly, by platform tooling or by institutional credentialism. Its justification is not that it escapes capture, but that its capture can remain the more visible and the more correctable — provided the procedures are kept lightweight, open, inspectable, local-first, plural, contestable and versioned.

**Evidence level:** design warning / internal consistency.

### Objection 6 — Bad actors can forge traces or manipulate provenance.

**Response.** Yes. For that very reason Reality Safety cannot rest on a single layer. It requires layered defences: cryptographic provenance where it is useful, social trust, institutional review, local memory, independent archives, challenge mechanisms and public objections. The claim envelope of section 22 separates, explicitly, the cryptographically verifiable fields from the socially attested and the declarative-only ones — precisely because a trace is not, of itself, true.

**Evidence level:** security / governance.

### Objection 7 — The public will not follow complex procedures.

**Response.** The public should not be required to inspect every trace. The aim is not to maximize the cognitive burden; the aim is resumability — that anyone, or any authorized reviewer, be able to return to the source and audit it when the need arises.

**Evidence level:** usability / institutional design.

### Objection 8 — Cynicism is sometimes justified.

**Response.** Yes. Cynicism may well expose corruption. But a generalized cynicism disables correction itself. The target here is not suspicion; it is nihilistic closure. And this remains a Level-B implication, philosophical and sociological, not the evidential foundation of the Reality Safety thesis.

**Evidence level:** philosophical / political.

## 15. Levels of evidence

### Level A — Established facts

- AI systems can generate text, image, audio and video at scale.
- AI-generated or AI-manipulated content can blur origin, authorship and status.
- Public policy now includes transparency obligations for synthetic content.
- Provenance standards such as C2PA exist and are being developed/adopted.
- AI Safety reports and institutions now treat general-purpose AI risk as a major policy domain.

### Level B — Defensible interpretations

- AI Safety has broadened from catastrophic risk toward systemic, societal and institutional risk.
- Synthetic media transparency addresses part of the problem but not the whole problem.
- Provenance is necessary but insufficient without correction, responsibility and source/form/platform discipline.
- AI intensifies an already existing crisis of shared reality.

### Level C — Corpus-specific hypotheses

- “Reality Safety” is the deeper category of which AI Safety is currently a revealer.
- The Ubik effect is a useful diagnostic concept for reality dislocation.
- Procedural stabilizers are the democratic stabilizing tendency whose failure modes can be made more visible, contestable and correctable than magical, authoritarian, market or cynical stabilizers.
- Cogentia and Ubikia can become practical components of Reality Safety.
- Fractanet can extend Reality Safety from documents to distributed AI-first infrastructures.

### Level D — Sources of inspiration / analogies

- Philip K. Dick’s *Ubik* as literary laboratory of reality dislocation.
- Orwell’s *1984* as authoritarian stabilization through trace control.
- *Ghost in the Shell* as artistic exploration of the biological/mechanical/informational boundary.
- Cybernetics, homeostasis and allostasis as models for viability-oriented systems.
- Git and version control as practical stabilizers of process memory.

## 19. Design principles for Reality Safety systems

A system claiming to support Reality Safety should obey at least the following principles.

### 19.1 Source sovereignty

The source corpus must remain distinct from publications and platform appearances.

### 19.2 Traceable transformation

Every meaningful transformation should be traceable:

```text
source → draft → critique → revision → derived product → publication → feedback → corpus reintegration
```

### 19.3 Verifiability classes

The system should distinguish cryptographically verifiable facts, socially attested claims and declarative-only metadata. Mixing these categories recreates the Ubik effect inside the stabilizer itself.

### 19.4 Regime awareness

A system should distinguish ordinary, perturbed, degraded, critical, vital and recovery situations. The same claim or action may require different levels of review, trace, escalation and human responsibility depending on deadline, uncertainty, stakes and reversibility.

### 19.5 First-class objections

Objections must be stored as contributions, not treated as noise.

### 19.6 Human responsibility

Artificial agents may contribute epistemically. Living persons remain responsible for consequential publication and governance.

### 19.7 Locality and subsidiarity

Not every trace must become global. Reality Safety should be fractal:

```text
local memory
→ project memory
→ institutional memory
→ public memory
```

Only what must ascend should ascend.

### 19.8 Correction without erasure

Correction should not silently destroy the history of error.

### 19.9 Anti-monopoly

No single platform, model, institution or identity layer should become the sovereign stabilizer of reality.

### 19.10 Usability

Reality Safety must not impose impossible verification burdens on ordinary people. It must make audit possible, not mandatory for every act.

## 22. Claim envelope as first practical instrument

This document should not yet pretend to define a finished software tool. The concept is not mature enough, and no single example has yet been tested from source to public derivative and back to corpus. Naming a product too early would create a false sense of closure.

The more appropriate next object is a **claim envelope**.

A claim envelope is a small, explicit structure attached to a claim or derived product. It records enough information to preserve the relation between appearance, source, reasoning, transformation, responsibility and correction, without pretending to solve the entire Reality Safety problem.

The envelope must not treat all metadata as having the same evidential status. It should distinguish at least three classes:

| Class | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **V — cryptographically or computationally verifiable** | can be checked by computation or by a signed/hashed artifact | commit hash, content checksum, signed tag, timestamp, ledger entry id |
| **S — socially attested** | depends on accountable human or institutional declaration, review or mandate | responsible person, review status, accountable institution, correction duty |
| **D — declarative-only** | useful context, but not independently verified by the envelope itself | audience, intent, rhetorical adaptation, platform purpose |

The first version can remain deliberately small:

```yaml
claim_envelope:
  schema: reality_safety_claim_envelope
  version: 0.1
  claim_id:
    value:
    class: D
  claim_text:
    value:
    class: D
  evidence_level:
    value: A | B | C | D
    class: S
  source_refs:
    value:
    class: V | S
  source_commit:
    value:
    class: V
  source_checksum:
    value:
    class: V
  reasoning_steps:
    value:
    class: S
  transformations:
    value:
    class: S
  objections:
    value:
    class: S
  unresolved_objections:
    value:
    class: S
  responsible_person:
    value:
    class: S
  accountable_institution:
    value:
    class: S
  derived_product:
    value:
    class: D | S
  platform_appearance:
    value:
    class: D
  correction_channel:
    value:
    class: S
  return_to_corpus:
    value:
    class: S
  status:
    value: draft | reviewed | published | corrected | deprecated | disputed
    class: S
```

This is not yet a tool specification. It is a disciplined intermediate object. It allows the corpus to proceed in the right order:

```text
claims stabilized
→ claim envelopes
→ tested examples of transformation
→ publication envelopes
→ later tooling, if the examples justify it
```

The claim envelope demonstrates the core doctrine:

```text
Do not merely publish appearances.
Preserve the relation between appearance, source, reasoning and correction.
```

## 24. Closing formula

AI Safety asks how to make artificial intelligence less dangerous.

Reality Safety asks how to preserve a world in which danger, truth, error, responsibility and correction can still be meaningfully distinguished.

AI did not create the whole crisis. It reveals it. It accelerates it. It makes it impossible to ignore.

The response cannot be an oracle, a platform, a ministry, a watermark, a model, or a cynical shrug.

The response must be procedural:

```text
source
provenance
version
trace
objection
mandate
persona
responsibility
correction
return to corpus
```

Not a magical Ubik that would restore reality from above.

A democratic procedural Ubik that preserves the conditions under which reality remains corrigible.

Not one reality imposed from above.

A shared reality kept traceable, contestable and habitable enough to endure.
