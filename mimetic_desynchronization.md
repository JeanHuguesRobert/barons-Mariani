# Indirect Action Under Mimetic Constraints
## Toward a General Theory of Structural Change

**Abstract**

This paper proposes a general framework for understanding structural change in social systems. It argues that the primary obstacle to innovation is not technical feasibility or rational disagreement, but the stabilization of meaning under conditions of uncertainty. Building on a formal analysis of the DRSJ cycle — a four-stage mechanism of interpretive stabilization documented empirically in Mediterranean social contexts (Robert, 2026) — it introduces the concept of *mimetic desynchronization* as a method for enabling change without triggering systemic resistance. The term *mimetic* is used in the Girardian sense: resistance to novelty is not merely cognitive but mediated by the triangular structure of desire, in which an actor's success becomes threatening precisely because it is close and comparable. The framework treats resistance not as an anomaly, but as an intrinsic property of all social systems, and reframes effective action as the configuration of conditions under which change becomes self-propagating before interpretive equilibrium is restored.

---

## 1. Introduction

Structural change consistently encounters resistance across domains. This resistance is often attributed to inertia, conflicting interests, or imperfect information. While these factors play a role, they do not fully explain the systematic character of failure in otherwise viable innovations — nor the fact that resistance intensifies precisely when a new structure begins to succeed.

This paper advances a different hypothesis:

> *Resistance to change arises from the need of social systems to stabilize meaning in the presence of novelty.*

This claim is not merely theoretical. It rests on a documented empirical mechanism — the DRSJ cycle (Robert, 2026) — which formalizes the recurring transformation of observed success into a moral problem legitimizing informal sanction. The present paper generalizes that mechanism into a theory of action: if the DRSJ cycle describes *how* systems resist, mimetic desynchronization describes *how* actors can navigate that resistance without triggering it prematurely.

---

## 2. The Mechanism of Interpretive Stabilization: The DRSJ Cycle

Any novel structure creates an interpretive gap between what is observed and what is understood. This gap generates instability. To resolve it, systems engage in a four-stage sequence formalized by Robert (2026) as the **DRSJ cycle**:

| Stage | Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **D** | Denial of competence | The success is observed but its real causes remain cognitively inaccessible to peers |
| **R** | Causal reattribution | Alternative explanations emerge: favoritism, manipulation, hidden advantage |
| **S** | Moral suspicion | The situation is reframed as unjust, independently of any investigation |
| **J** | Justification of sanction | Disqualification or exclusion becomes morally legitimate |

Three properties of this cycle are analytically critical.

It is **cumulative**: each stage reinforces the next. Once moral suspicion is installed, counter-evidence is reinterpreted as further proof of concealment.

It is **self-validating**: the disqualification produces concrete effects — reluctance to contract, withheld recommendations, anticipatory suspicion — which can alter the target's trajectory and retrospectively produce "evidence" for the initial suspicion.

It is **socially distributed**: it requires no identifiable malicious actor. It emerges from the ordinary circulation of judgments in a social space, without intentional coordination.

This mechanism is domain-independent, scale-invariant, and emergent. It constitutes what this paper calls *interpretive stabilization*.

---

## 3. The Mimetic Structure of Resistance

The DRSJ cycle does not operate on a neutral social substrate. It is activated within what Girard (1961) calls a *mimetic* structure: desire is not spontaneous but mediated by a model-rival. In contexts of strong mutual knowledge — villages, professional communities, small institutions — the model is precisely the one who shares the context. The success of this "nearby other" is doubly threatening: desired and simultaneously judged illegitimate because desired.

This Girardian structure explains a feature of the DRSJ cycle that purely cognitive accounts cannot: why resistance intensifies with *proximity* and *comparability* rather than with distance. It is not the remote success of a stranger that triggers the cycle; it is the visible success of someone from the same circle.

Schoeck (1966) documented this dynamic as a universal social regulator — the *invidia* mechanism — functioning as a structural brake on differentiation and innovation in high-proximity communities. Dunning's research on metacognition adds a further dimension: when competence is genuinely inaccessible to peers — because it was formed elsewhere, through opaque pathways — unexplained success becomes not merely threatening but *cognitively unbearable*. Incomprehension precedes moral judgment; moral judgment then provides its justification (Haidt, 2001). The resulting sanction is experienced not as envy but as the restoration of moral order (Fehr & Gächter, 2002).

The use of *mimetic* in this paper therefore carries precise theoretical content: resistance is not merely informational but structurally generated by the triangular proximity between the innovator, the observer, and the shared social space they inhabit.

---

## 4. Information Compression and System Efficiency

From a systems perspective, the DRSJ cycle can also be understood as a form of information compression. Novelty increases dimensionality, uncertainty, and cognitive load. Systems respond by simplifying, reinterpreting, and reducing variance.

This process is not irrational. It is efficient under constraint. However, it systematically disadvantages structures that cannot be immediately integrated into existing interpretive frameworks — precisely those structures that are genuinely novel.

The compression dynamic explains why increasing visibility without modifying interpretive conditions tends to accelerate resistance rather than reduce it: more signal, processed through the same DRSJ filter, produces more output from the cycle.

---

## 5. Indirect Action

Direct approaches to change operate at the level of explicit content: argumentation, demonstration, negotiation. They assume that resistance is primarily cognitive or informational, and that correcting information will correct behavior.

This assumption is false under DRSJ conditions. When resistance emerges from interpretive stabilization, direct action is not merely ineffective — it is often counterproductive. Increased visibility without modified interpretive conditions accelerates the cycle.

*Indirect action* can be defined as:

> *the modification of the conditions under which interpretation occurs, rather than the content of interpretation itself.*

The object of indirect action is not to persuade. It is to alter the system dynamics before the DRSJ cycle completes. This requires acting on the temporal and relational structure of interpretation, not on its propositional content.

---

## 6. Mimetic Desynchronization

This paper introduces *mimetic desynchronization* as the general method of indirect action.

**Definition.** Mimetic desynchronization is the deliberate decoupling of perception, interpretation, and reaction in order to prevent premature stabilization of meaning — specifically, to prevent the completion of the DRSJ cycle before the new structure has reached its irreversibility threshold.

In most social systems, these processes are tightly coupled:

- perception triggers interpretation  
- interpretation triggers judgment  
- judgment triggers sanction

Desynchronization alters this sequence by introducing structural delays or buffers at each transition.

The empirical basis for this concept is not merely theoretical. Robert (2026) documents a protocol of *non-ostentation* in Corsican social contexts, which functions precisely as a form of spontaneous mimetic desynchronization: by avoiding competitive signaling, the successful actor deprives the DRSJ cycle of its trigger — the perceived challenge to the mimetic order. The cycle is not eliminated but suspended, conditionally on discretion. What observers from outside the community have often misread as submission or erasure is, under this analysis, a sophisticated social strategy: the management of interpretive timing.

---

## 7. Mechanisms of Desynchronization

Several mechanisms contribute to mimetic desynchronization:

**Delayed visibility.** Postponing the point at which a new structure becomes perceptible reduces the lead time available for interpretive stabilization to complete.

**Interpretive buffering.** Reducing perceived discontinuity between the new structure and existing frameworks lowers the interpretive pressure that activates the DRSJ cycle. Framing novelty as extension rather than rupture is a canonical instance.

**Semantic minimization.** Reducing cognitive load — simplicity of presentation, modular introduction — slows the cascade from observation to judgment.

**Non-ostentation.** Avoiding competitive signaling removes the mimetic trigger. This mechanism is most effective in high-proximity, high-interconnection contexts, where the Girardian model-rival dynamic is most active.

**Reversibility.** Reducing the perceived structural stakes of adoption — making entry low-cost and exit possible — lowers the activation threshold for judgment. What can be undone need not be resisted immediately.

**Institutional pre-legitimation.** Robert (2026) notes that recognition by an external legitimating party — a diploma, a certification, an international award — can short-circuit the DRSJ cycle by making competence socially visible *before* suspicion installs itself. This is the institutional analog of mimetic desynchronization: the external signal reconfigures the interpretive frame without requiring the actor to navigate the local cycle.

These mechanisms do not eliminate resistance; they delay or diffuse its activation, buying time for the new structure to reach its irreversibility threshold.

---

## 8. Gradient-Based Dynamics

All systems evolve along gradients: energy in physical systems, survival in biological ones, utility in economic systems, legitimacy and coherence in social ones.

Effective action aligns with these gradients. Actors tend to follow paths of least resistance within the available structure. Mimetic desynchronization is, in part, the art of *reorienting gradients* — making adoption easier than rejection, acceptance safer than opposition, understanding simpler than questioning.

The gradient metaphor also clarifies why resistance cannot be permanently neutralized by will or argument: the gradient is structural, not propositional. Changing it requires reconfiguring the field, not persuading its inhabitants.

---

## 9. Irreversibility and Phase Transition

Structural change exhibits threshold behavior. Below a certain point, the DRSJ cycle dominates: resistance is cheaper than acceptance. Above this point, normalization occurs: the new structure is integrated into the existing interpretive framework, often retrospectively legitimized.

The **irreversibility point** can be defined as:

> *the moment at which reverting to the previous state becomes more costly than accepting the new one.*

This is analogous to a phase transition in physical systems — and to the moment at which, in Robert's (2026) formulation, the DRSJ cycle is "bouclé": once the sanction is applied and produces concrete effects, the cycle becomes self-sustaining. Mimetic desynchronization aims to prevent completion of the cycle before the irreversibility point is reached from the *other side* — the side of adoption rather than rejection.

The general principle can be stated as:

> *Structural change succeeds when transformation precedes stabilization of meaning.*

Or equivalently: *effective action alters system dynamics before interpretive equilibrium is restored.*

---

## 10. Implications

This framework applies across contexts: technological adoption, organizational change, institutional transformation, social innovation, political reform. It suggests that:

- resistance is intrinsic, not exceptional, and its mechanism is the DRSJ cycle operating under mimetic conditions;
- persuasion is secondary to configuration — the object of action is the interpretive field, not its current inhabitants;
- legitimacy is typically *retrospective*, produced after the irreversibility threshold is crossed rather than before it;
- the most effective actors in high-proximity, high-resistance environments are those who have internalized mimetic desynchronization as a practice, often without formalizing it as such.

---

## 11. Conclusion

The central difficulty of change lies not in invention, but in integration. The DRSJ cycle — a universal mechanism of interpretive stabilization, documented with particular clarity in Mediterranean social contexts — explains why viable innovations fail not on technical grounds but on interpretive ones.

By grounding resistance in a formal, named mechanism rather than in vague appeals to inertia or self-interest, this paper opens a precise question: at which stage of the DRSJ cycle is intervention most effective, and by which mechanism of desynchronization?

The answer will be context-dependent. But the framework is general. To change a system, one must act on the conditions that produce interpretation, not on the interpretations themselves — and one must act *before the cycle completes*.

Mimetic desynchronization is the name for that action.
