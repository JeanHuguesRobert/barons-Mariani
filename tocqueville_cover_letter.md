**To the Editors,**  
*European Journal of Social Theory*

I am pleased to submit for your consideration the article **"The Generalized Tocqueville Law: Progress, Rising Expectations, and the Structural Production of Dissatisfaction"** (approximately 11,400 words, main text and notes).

**The contribution.** The paper proposes a unified theoretical mechanism — the Generalized Tocqueville Law — to account for a paradox that is simultaneously well-attested empirically and poorly understood analytically: that objective improvements in safety, institutional quality, and welfare consistently generate intensifying rather than diminishing perceptions of failure and injustice. While Tocqueville identified this dynamic in its original context (the French Revolution), and while subsequent sociological work — Gurr's relative deprivation model, Boudon's political mobilisation law, the Easterlin paradox — has identified related mechanisms in specific domains, no unified formulation applicable across domains has been developed.

The paper makes three contributions. First, it identifies four constitutive mechanisms — tolerance threshold displacement, normative horizon elevation, relative frustration, and structural amnesia — that together produce the Tocqueville dynamic across analytically distinct social settings. Second, it introduces a *perceived severity index* S(t) = Δ(t)/(1 − Q(t)) — the residual deficit relative to the remaining distance to perfection — which Appendix A shows to be strictly increasing under a tractable parametric family of threshold functions. This formalises Tocqueville's claim that "sensibility grows keener even as the evil diminishes" in mathematically precise terms and renders the law falsifiable. Third, it extends the framework beyond its traditional domains of revolution and political mobilisation to high-reliability technical systems and digital content moderation — thereby demonstrating cross-domain generality while contributing to literatures (sociology of technology, platform governance) that have not engaged with this theoretical tradition.

**The three case studies** are chosen to triangulate across analytically distinct domains: aviation/medicine (physical infrastructure, stochastic failure), digital content moderation (sociotechnical system, adversarial dynamics — illustrated by the 160-fold increase in NCMEC CyberTipline reports between 2010 and 2023 driven by improved detection rather than increasing harm), and Corsican nationalist mobilisation (political recognition, recursive identity logic). Five figures, including a formal diagram of the mechanisms and a visualisation of the co-evolution of Q(t), τ(t), and S(t), accompany the text.

**Scope conditions and falsifiability.** Section 7.1 develops a null model specifying three conditions under which the mechanism does not activate, each generating a falsifiable prediction. This addresses the principal theoretical weakness of prior formulations (Gurr, Boudon), which lack explicit scope conditions.

**Fit with the journal.** *European Journal of Social Theory* has published work at the intersection of formal sociological theory and empirical case analysis — including contributions by Boudon, Hirschman's intellectual heirs, and scholars working on the sociology of expectations. This paper contributes to that tradition while extending it to contemporary domains (platform governance, minority recognition) and enriching it with a formal index not previously developed in this literature.
