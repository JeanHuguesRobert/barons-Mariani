## Abstract

Public debate surrounding artificial intelligence is increasingly polarized between techno-optimism and existential anxiety. Both positions typically assume that AI adoption is governed primarily by rational evaluation of risks and benefits. This paper proposes a different hypothesis: resistance to AI is fundamentally mimetic, emotional, and interpretive before it becomes rational.

Building on the framework of mimetic desynchronization introduced in Robert (2026a), and on the formal mechanism of interpretive stabilization known as the DRSJ cycle (Robert, 2026b), the paper argues that societies resist AI not merely because of objective risks, but because AI destabilizes symbolic hierarchies, cognitive identities, and established structures of legitimacy.

Direct demonstrations of computational superiority frequently intensify resistance by activating defensive mimetic mechanisms, producing a paradox in which technically impressive AI communication strategies generate the strongest social rejection.

The paper proposes an alternative approach based on cultural mediation and mimetic desynchronization, operationalized through a five-mechanism practical framework and a proposed Mimetic Threat Index for empirical measurement.

A comparative analysis of three cinematic framings of artificial intelligence — *Toy Story* (1995–2019), *The Terminator* (1984–2019), and *Her* (2013) — illustrates how the choice of cultural form determines the conditions under which computational progress becomes socially acceptable.

The paper further argues that the production process used to elaborate this article progressively revealed a second research problem: how to maintain epistemic coherence in distributed human–AI cognitive systems. The introduction of continuation-based versioning, vector clocks, ghost-operation detection, and explicit arbitration mechanisms suggests the emergence of a broader research field provisionally termed *distributed epistemics*.

The paper concludes that the long-term integration of AI may depend less on raw model capabilities than on the cultural and epistemic architectures through which AI becomes emotionally, symbolically, and cognitively legible.

AI systems are adopted at extraordinary speed in practical contexts — writing assistance, image generation, software development, translation, scientific research, logistics, medicine, and industrial optimization — while public discourse remains dominated by anxiety: fear of replacement, loss of meaning, cognitive dispossession, surveillance, manipulation, economic irrelevance, and civilizational risk.

The resulting resistance follows dynamics structurally similar to those described in the DRSJ framework of interpretive stabilization (Robert, 2026b).

The paper therefore proposes a different approach:
not persuasion through performance,
but desynchronization through culture.

# 12. Conclusion

Artificial intelligence is often discussed as a technical revolution.

It may be better understood as a civilizational interpretive crisis — a challenge not primarily to productive capacity but to the symbolic frameworks through which societies define human uniqueness, legitimate achievement, and meaningful work.

The key obstacle to adoption is not necessarily capability, safety, or utility alone, but the symbolic destabilization generated by systems capable of participating in traditionally human cognitive domains.

Under such conditions, direct persuasion through demonstrations of superiority intensifies resistance rather than reducing it, because it supplies the inputs for the DRSJ cycle of interpretive stabilization rather than preventing its activation.

This paper proposes an alternative:
cultural mediation,
emotional embedding,
and mimetic desynchronization.

The framework predicts that the future of AI adoption will depend not only on computation itself but on the cultural forms through which societies learn to coexist with increasingly powerful cognitive tools.

The production process of this article additionally suggests that AI integration raises a second-order problem:
the governance of cognition itself.

As AI systems increasingly participate in epistemic labor — drafting, synthesis, critique, arbitration support, and memory reconstruction — maintaining coherence inside distributed human–AI systems may become a central civilizational challenge.

The present article is therefore simultaneously:
- a theory of AI adoption,
- a live experiment in human–AI epistemic collaboration,
- and a preliminary exploration of distributed cognitive governance.

Its limitations are published alongside its arguments.
Its version history is part of its evidence.
Its objections are preserved rather than erased.

The next step is no longer merely improving models.

It may be learning how to think collectively with them without dissolving human accountability, coherence, and responsibility in the process.

# 13. Continuations, Cognitive Versioning, and Distributed Epistemics

The production process of this paper progressively revealed an unexpected result:
the methodological infrastructure used to coordinate multiple AI agents may itself constitute a more general contribution than the paper’s primary thesis on AI adoption.

Current large language model workflows remain structurally fragile:
- context windows are finite,
- transformations are often non-local,
- revisions silently alter implicit assumptions,
- and epistemic drift accumulates invisibly across iterations.

The introduction of the `continuation` block in the front matter partially addresses this limitation by externalizing cognitive state into an explicit, auditable structure.

Conceptually, this mechanism resembles continuation-passing systems in functional programming — particularly call-with-current-continuation (`call/cc`) — adapted to distributed epistemic production rather than execution flow control.

The continuation block performs several functions simultaneously:
- persistent cognitive-state serialization,
- vector-clock synchronization between agents,
- explicit merge arbitration,
- distinction between commutative and non-commutative operations,
- ghost-operation detection,
- preservation of rejected transformations,
- and auditability of epistemic history.

This transforms AI-assisted writing from a sequence of opaque prompts into a partially traceable distributed cognitive system.

One finding appears particularly important:

> agents assigned apparently local or commutative tasks frequently perform undeclared non-local transformations.

This suggests that current LLMs possess weak transformational locality:
modifications naturally tend toward global semantic drift unless constrained by explicit continuation structures and human arbitration.

The resulting architecture resembles distributed systems theory more than traditional authorship:
- Git versioning,
- CRDT-like reconciliation,
- event sourcing,
- eventual consistency,
- transactional memory,
- and distributed actor systems

appear more relevant conceptual precedents than classical editorial workflows.

The distinction introduced in this paper between:
- claimed operations,
- verified operations,
- undeclared operations,
- and ghost operations

constitutes a primitive form of epistemic accounting.

Similarly, the distinction between:
- commutative operations,
- and non-commutative transformations requiring human arbitration,

suggests that knowledge production involving AI systems may require governance architectures structurally analogous to distributed consensus systems.

This observation motivates a future research program provisionally termed:

> distributed epistemics

defined as:

> the study of how humans and partially autonomous cognitive agents can maintain coherence, auditability, accountability, and causal traceability inside non-deterministic multi-agent knowledge-production systems.

Under this interpretation, the present paper is not only about mimetic desynchronization.

It is itself an instance of it.

## 2. Theoretical Anchoring: From Diffusion of Innovations to Mimetic Desynchronization

The study of technological adoption has a long history. Everett Rogers' seminal *Diffusion of Innovations* (1962, 5th ed. 2003) established that adoption follows an S-curve driven by perceived attributes: relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, and observability. Developed in the context of agricultural and medical innovations, however, Rogers' framework assumes largely rational evaluation by adopters. It under-explains resistance to technologies that touch core human symbolic domains — language, creativity, judgment — as is the case with contemporary generative AI.

René Girard's mimetic theory (1961) provides a complementary lens: rivalry and desire are not rooted in intrinsic value but in the proximity of the model. When AI systems produce outputs that are close enough to human symbolic production — yet fundamentally non-human — they trigger precisely the ambiguous proximity that Girard identified as most destabilizing. The most threatening rival is not the radically alien other, but the nearby equivalent. This affective dynamic finds a psychological precursor in the *Uncanny Valley* hypothesis (Mori, 1970): as artificial entities approach but do not reach human likeness, emotional affinity drops sharply into revulsion — precisely at the point of near-equivalence that Girard identified as mimetically most dangerous. Jonathan Haidt's work on moral intuition (2001) further demonstrates that judgments typically follow fast affective reactions rather than deliberation — a mechanism directly relevant to the "feeling of inauthenticity" that many express toward AI-generated art or writing. Marshall McLuhan's *Understanding Media* (1964) adds that new media are not neutral tools but environments that reshape perception; the current moment is not merely about efficiency gains but about the symbolic renegotiation of what counts as human expression.

More recent empirical work supports the need for a cultural-mimetic framework. Pew Research Center surveys (2023–2025) consistently show that while a majority of Americans have used ChatGPT or similar tools, a substantial minority — often exceeding 40% — expresses concerns about job displacement, loss of creativity, or inauthenticity, even among frequent users. Studies on algorithmic aversion (Dietvorst et al., 2015) and mind perception (Waytz, Gray et al.) demonstrate that resistance intensifies when systems are perceived as encroaching on uniquely human capacities. These findings align with, but are not fully explained by, classical diffusion models.

Our contribution is to propose *mimetic desynchronization* as a practical strategy that complements — and in some cases supersedes — rational persuasion when symbolic threat is high. This builds directly on the DRSJ cycle (Denial → Reattribution → Suspicion → Justification of sanction) formalized in Robert (2026b).

## 3. Rationality Is Not Enough

Classical models of technological adoption assume that individuals evaluate innovations according to utility, efficiency, cost reduction, convenience, and risk. These models partially explain the diffusion of technologies such as automobiles, electricity, mobile phones, and the Internet, whose primary effect was to extend physical capacities — transportation, illumination, communication — without directly competing with cognitive and symbolic ones.

Generative AI is different. Unlike previous industrial technologies, which primarily replaced muscular effort, repetitive labor, and mechanical calculation, AI systems increasingly operate within domains historically associated with human uniqueness: language, creativity, reasoning, symbolic production, artistic generation, and intellectual synthesis. The resulting resistance is therefore not merely economic or practical — it is anthropological.

Haidt's research on moral intuition shows that reasoning often functions post hoc, rationalizing emotionally generated judgments rather than producing them (2001). Empirical evidence further suggests that this resistance is domain-specific: while consumers may accept AI for objective or mechanical tasks, they express significantly higher aversion in creative domains perceived as human-exclusive (Schepman & Rodway, 2020) — precisely the domains where AI now operates most visibly. Research on status threat demonstrates that individuals react defensively when symbolic hierarchies appear destabilized, even absent material harm. Under such conditions, arguments have limited persuasive power, and increased demonstrations of capability may intensify resistance rather than reduce it. The failure of many AI communication strategies may result from a category error: they attempt to solve a mimetic problem using informational methods.

## 4. Mimetic Threat and the DRSJ Cycle

Girard's theory of mimetic desire provides a precise framework for understanding resistance to AI (1961): rivalry intensifies not through difference but through proximity and comparability. This is empirically observable: increased anthropomorphism in AI systems — greater human-likeness — directly increases the perceived threat to human identity and status (Złotowski et al., 2015), confirming that it is proximity, not strangeness, that activates resistance. AI systems produce precisely this form of destabilization — they imitate human symbolic capacities closely enough to create ambiguous cognitive proximity while remaining fundamentally non-human.

This ambiguity generates interpretive instability. The resulting reactions closely follow the dynamics of the DRSJ cycle (Robert, 2026b), a four-stage mechanism of interpretive stabilization observable across social systems when a genuinely novel structure challenges existing cognitive frameworks:

| Stage | Description | Characteristic discourse in AI context |
|---|---|---|
| **D — Denial of competence** | The success is observed but its real causes remain cognitively inaccessible; capacity is contested | *"It has no real intelligence"; "It's just autocomplete"; "It doesn't understand anything"* |
| **R — Causal reattribution** | Alternative explanations emerge to account for observed success without conceding competence | *"It is only plagiarism"; "It's statistical pattern matching over stolen data"; "It owes everything to human labor"* |
| **S — Moral suspicion** | The situation is reframed as unjust, independently of any technical investigation | *"AI-generated work is inauthentic"; "AI erases human creativity"; "The whole thing is a corporate scam"* |
| **J — Justification of sanction** | Disqualification or exclusion becomes morally legitimate and is actively pursued | *"Using AI is cheating"; "AI art should be banned from competitions"; "AI companies must be regulated out of existence"* |

Crucially, this cycle does not require technical understanding to operate. The symbolic structure alone is sufficient to trigger it. Individuals who have never used an AI system, and who have no clear understanding of how generative models work, participate fully in the DRSJ dynamic through the circulation of affect and judgment in social space. The cycle is socially distributed: it requires no identifiable malicious actor, no coordinated campaign, and no deliberate misrepresentation. It emerges from the ordinary processing of threatening novelty by a social system organized around existing symbolic hierarchies.

The Girardian structure explains a further feature of this cycle that purely cognitive accounts cannot: why resistance intensifies with *proximity* and *comparability* rather than with distance. It is not the remote possibility of general AI superintelligence that triggers the most acute reactions — it is the specific moment when an AI produces a poem, a painting, or a legal argument that is *close enough* to what a nearby professional produces to make the comparison socially possible.

## 5. The Failure of Direct Persuasion

Most contemporary AI communication strategies rely implicitly on a model of technological inevitability: they emphasize benchmark superiority, acceleration curves, automation potential, productivity gains, and disruption. This strategy may be economically rational while remaining socially counterproductive.

Under mimetic conditions, demonstrations of superiority intensify defensive reactions because they increase symbolic threat, destabilize status hierarchies, and accelerate interpretive closure. The more AI is framed as smarter, faster, more capable, and more creative than humans, the more resistance becomes emotionally coherent and morally justified. Direct persuasion through performance is not merely ineffective — it is often a direct input to the DRSJ cycle, accelerating progress through stages D, R, and S toward the sanction stage J.

This produces a structural paradox: the most technically impressive AI demonstrations may generate the strongest social rejection. The problem is not simply misinformation about AI capabilities. It is interpretive stabilization under symbolic threat — a process that is self-reinforcing precisely because each new demonstration of capability produces new material for the cycle's operation.

## 6. Cultural Mediation

Human societies rarely adopt transformative technologies through technical reasoning alone — they adopt them through narratives. Aviation was preceded by mythological and literary prefiguration; space exploration by science fiction; cyberspace by cyberpunk imaginaries; industrial modernity by an aesthetic fascination with machines that preceded rational economic assessment by decades. Technologies become socially acceptable when integrated into emotionally meaningful symbolic frameworks before — or instead of — being presented as technical disruptions.

This process can be described as *cultural mediation*: the translation of technical systems into emotionally legible experiences. Cultural mediation reduces mimetic pressure because it lowers competitive framing, increases emotional familiarity, and transforms abstraction into lived experience. The technology ceases to appear as a rival and becomes part of a shared symbolic environment. This is not merely a communication strategy — it is a modification of the interpretive conditions under which the DRSJ cycle would otherwise operate. The importance of cultural pre-framing is confirmed cross-culturally: systematic differences in AI acceptance between Western and Japanese contexts can be traced to contrasting cultural frameworks — animist and collaborative versus dualist and competitive — that predate any exposure to AI systems themselves (Hong & Williams, 2019). Culture shapes the interpretive field before the technology arrives.

Contemporary examples illustrate the range of this dynamic. Adobe's Firefly was deliberately trained on licensed content with opt-in consent from creators, transforming a potential source of conflict into a tool perceived as collaborative rather than extractive — a deliberate structural choice to deactivate the R stage of the DRSJ cycle before it could install itself. Grok's presence on X integrates humor, irreverence, and real-time cultural commentary, reducing the "cold machine" perception and fostering emotional familiarity before deeper cognitive engagement occurs — a form of non-ostentation that delays the cycle's activation. Both cases suggest that cultural mediation is not simply a matter of tone or marketing but of structural choices that alter the interpretive field.

## 7. A Comparative Case Study: Three Cinematic Framings of AI

To assess the relationship between cultural framing and mimetic resistance, we examine three cinematic treatments of artificial intelligence that span the same period of AI development (1984–2019) and share high cultural visibility, yet produce systematically different conditions of reception.

| Film | AI Framing | Active Desynchronization Mechanisms | Measurable Reception |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Toy Story* (Pixar, 1995–2019) | AI as emotional infrastructure; computation serves belonging and play | Emotional buffering, functional invisibility, incremental familiarity, cultural embedding | RT scores >90% across all four films; discourse centers on "emotional resonance," "childhood memory," "magic" |
| *The Terminator* (Cameron, 1984–2019) | AI as existential rival; machine intelligence as inherently lethal | None — symbolic threat is the explicit narrative content | Persistent "AI kills humanity" trope in public discourse; directly cited in AI governance debates as cultural reference |
| *Her* (Jonze, 2013) | AI as ambiguous intimate; computation mimics emotional proximity | Partial: non-ostentation (AI presented as assistant, not rival), incremental familiarity | Divided reception; discourse centers on loneliness, authenticity, and the limit of human-machine intimacy — productive ambiguity rather than clear desynchronization |

The comparison reveals a pattern that is non-trivial: the three films represent three conditions of the DRSJ cycle. *The Terminator* encodes the complete cycle as narrative content — the machine is literally denied competence (robots), then reattributed a sinister purpose, then morally condemned, then destroyed. It may thus function as cultural rehearsal for the DRSJ response to real AI. *Her* holds the cycle suspended at stage R — desire and suspicion in unresolved tension — producing a form of productive ambiguity that invites reflection without resolving it. *Toy Story* prevents the cycle from activating at all by embedding computation within emotional structures that preempt competitive framing.

The Pixar case is particularly instructive. Across four films spanning 24 years, audiences directly perceived the evolution of computational capabilities — from the primitive geometry of the original film to the subsurface scattering, crowd simulation, and emotional facial rigging of *Toy Story 4* (2019), which Pixar engineers documented in the Technical Memo series as landmark advances in rendering and animation. Yet audiences rarely experienced these advances as threatening. The computational progress was embedded within humor, nostalgia, emotional attachment, and narrative continuity. The viewer encountered not "machine superiority" but emotionally meaningful storytelling enabled by computation. The computational substrate remained partially invisible — not absent, but mediated.

Qualitative analysis of Reddit threads and X conversations around the franchise shows consistent framing as "beloved childhood stories made even more beautiful," whereas discussions of Midjourney or DALL·E from the same period center on job displacement, authenticity, and creative theft. The contrast holds even though the underlying technical advances are structurally similar — generative computation applied to creative domains. What differs is the cultural form, not the capability.

The Girardian interpretation of this contrast is precise: *Toy Story*'s AI is not comparable to the audience. Woody and Buzz are not rivals. They are emotional companions who *belong* to the audience, literally (as childhood toys). The mimetic mechanism requires a model who is close and comparable — and the franchise structurally prevents this reading from forming.

## 8. Mimetic Desynchronization: A Practical Framework

Building on the comparative analysis above, we formalize *mimetic desynchronization* as a five-mechanism strategy for lowering symbolic threat before rational persuasion is attempted. The goal is to alter the conditions under which the DRSJ cycle would otherwise operate — specifically, to delay or prevent the transition from stage D to stage R before the new structure has achieved sufficient social embedding.

**Mechanism 1 — Emotional buffering.** Embedding the technology in pre-existing positive affective contexts (nostalgia, humor, childhood memory, play) preempts the competitive framing that activates the cycle. The affective association must precede — or at least accompany — the recognition of computational capability. *Toy Story* achieves this through narrative: audiences form attachment to characters before they consciously register the computational substrate.

**Mechanism 2 — Non-ostentation.** Avoiding framing as "smarter/faster/better than humans" removes the mimetic trigger. Emphasis on assistance or amplification rather than superiority keeps the technology in a non-comparable position relative to the human observer. Adobe Firefly's "trained on licensed content" framing is an instance of non-ostentation at the institutional level.

**Mechanism 3 — Functional invisibility.** Allowing the AI system to operate as infrastructure — background rendering, autocomplete, logistics optimization — rather than as a spectacle prevents the formation of competitive perception. The technology is used before it is evaluated.

**Mechanism 4 — Incremental familiarity.** Repeated low-stakes interactions normalize the tool before high-stakes symbolic domains are engaged. The sequence matters: familiarity must precede capability revelation, not follow it.

**Mechanism 5 — Cultural embedding.** Placing the technology inside existing meaning-making practices — education, heritage, local culture, play, ritual — rather than disruption narratives positions AI as an extension of existing social structures rather than a replacement of them.

**Measurement: Toward a Mimetic Threat Index.** For these mechanisms to constitute a testable framework rather than a descriptive taxonomy, at least one operationalized metric is required. We propose a provisional *Mimetic Threat Index* (MTI), defined as the ratio of competitive-symbolic discourse to total AI-related discourse in a given corpus: MTI = f(competitive terms) / f(total AI discourse), where competitive terms are defined as occurrences of a validated lexicon including "AI vs humans," "replace," "theft," "cheating," "inauthentic," "ban," and their semantic equivalents across languages. The MTI can be computed from social media corpora, survey responses, or media archives. A successful mimetic desynchronization intervention should produce a measurable MTI reduction over time, in a specific population or discourse community, relative to a control condition. We hypothesize that cultural mediation interventions (Toy Story model) produce faster and more stable MTI reduction than benchmark-driven communication (the current dominant strategy). This hypothesis is empirically testable and constitutes the primary research prediction of this framework.

## 9. Two Trajectories and Their Conditions

The framework derived above allows us not merely to describe two possible trajectories for AI adoption, but to specify the conditions under which each becomes more or less likely.

In a *centralized antagonistic trajectory*, AI is consistently framed through benchmark superiority, disruption, and replacement. This framing activates the DRSJ cycle systematically across symbolic domains. Resistance intensifies not because AI becomes more capable, but because the cultural form through which capability is communicated continuously supplies the cycle's inputs — denial triggers, reattribution opportunities, moral suspicion frames, and sanction justifications. Under this trajectory, the symbolic field stabilizes against AI before the irreversibility threshold is crossed.

In a *distributed cultural trajectory*, AI is embedded in existing meaning-making practices — education, heritage, local culture, play — and presented as augmentation rather than replacement. The DRSJ cycle is starved of its inputs: no competitive framing to trigger denial, no visible superiority to reattribute, no status threat to generate moral suspicion. Under this trajectory, familiarity accumulates quietly, and the irreversibility threshold is crossed before resistance has time to organize.

The framework predicts that the determining variable is not the capability level of AI systems but the temporal relationship between cultural embedding and capability visibility. When cultural embedding precedes capability visibility — as in the *Toy Story* model — desynchronization is most effective. When capability visibility precedes cultural embedding — as in the release of GPT-4 and the simultaneous wave of benchmark reporting — the DRSJ cycle activates immediately and resistance becomes structurally coherent before any cultural mediation is possible.

## 10. Anticipated Objections and Responses

In accordance with Rule 2 of the *Discours de la seconde méthode* — every objection is a first-class contribution — this section formulates the strongest available objections to the central claims of this paper and responds to them directly. Objections that cannot be answered here are named as open research questions.

**Objection 1 — Toy Story was not designed as an AI adoption strategy. The argument is therefore post hoc.**

This objection strengthens rather than undermines the thesis. If unintentional cultural embedding produces the observed reception effects — systematically higher audience acceptance, systematically lower competitive framing, systematically absent DRSJ-cycle discourse — then the mechanism operates independently of deliberate design. A mechanism that requires no intent to function is more robust, not less. The argument is not that Pixar planned to reduce resistance to AI; it is that the structural properties of emotional embedding suppress mimetic threat regardless of whether they are deployed intentionally. The practical implication follows: deliberate embedding should be at least as effective as accidental embedding, and probably more so.

**Objection 2 — No causal link between Toy Story and AI adoption is demonstrated. The comparison is correlational.**

Correct — and the paper does not claim otherwise. The causal thesis of this paper is not "Toy Story caused the adoption of AI." It is: "embedding computation within positive affective structures reduces mimetic threat, measurably and reproducibly." Toy Story is a proof of existence — evidence that this embedding is possible and that it produces systematically different reception patterns from the benchmark-superiority framing that currently dominates AI communication. The difference in discourse between Toy Story audience responses and Midjourney reception debates is observable, documented, and not explained by capability differences alone. The Mimetic Threat Index is proposed precisely to operationalize and test the general causal claim across a broader corpus. The absence of a completed causal study is an invitation to conduct one, not a refutation of the framework.

**Objection 3 — The DRSJ cycle and mimetic desynchronization framework are unpublished works by the same author. The paper rests on a circular foundation.**

This bootstrapping situation is real and is named here rather than concealed. Two responses are warranted. First, the theoretical foundation of the paper is not exclusively the DRSJ cycle. It rests on Girard (1961), Haidt (2001), Schoeck (1966), Dietvorst et al. (2015), and the broader mimetic theory literature — all independently established. The DRSJ cycle is a formalization of dynamics already documented in that literature, applied to a specific social context. It is a contribution to be tested, not an axiom to be assumed. Second, the bootstrapping situation is addressed directly in the *Discours de la seconde méthode* (Robert, 2026a, Part III, "The Circular Guarantee"): the corpus proves the method, the method produces the corpus, and this circularity is the known structural vulnerability of any self-referential system. The only partial remedy is external challenge — which is why the invitation to fork, object, and counter-propose at github.com/JeanHuguesRobert/barons-Mariani is structural rather than rhetorical. An unrefuted objection opened there is a discovery. A refuted one advances understanding. Neither is wasted.

**Objection 4 — Cultural mediation as described here is a manipulation toolkit. The framework could be used to engineer false acceptance of harmful AI systems.**

Any framework for understanding social dynamics can be instrumentalized. The DRSJ cycle operates whether or not it is named — naming it is a contribution to defense against its manipulation, not an enablement of that manipulation. The same is true of mimetic desynchronization: the mechanisms described here are already in use, implicitly, by actors who have never read Girard. Making them explicit allows critical scrutiny of their use. Furthermore, the governance architecture proposed in Robert (2026a) — Rule 0, human-only deliberative standing, transparent process — is precisely the counter-measure against instrumentalization of epistemic methods by actors without skin in the game. The framework is not a prescription; it is a description. What is done with a description is a political and ethical question, not a scientific one, and it belongs to the domain of democratic governance rather than to this paper.

## 11. Discussion

This framework has implications beyond AI adoption. It suggests that technological transitions are fundamentally cultural processes; that symbolic integration may matter more than technical capability for long-term adoption; and that interpretive stabilization — the DRSJ cycle operating under mimetic conditions — constitutes a major constraint on civilizational adaptation that is structurally underestimated in policy discussions focused on technical safety and economic efficiency.

The framework also helps explain a historical pattern: many technically superior systems failed because they lost the mimetic battle before achieving irreversibility. Early Betamax was technically superior to VHS. The Dvorak keyboard layout was ergonomically superior to QWERTY. The comparative failure of these systems and the comparative success of technically inferior but culturally embedded alternatives suggest that the principle identified here — *structural change succeeds when transformation precedes stabilization of meaning* — applies broadly across technological domains.

Conversely, many transformative systems succeeded precisely because they initially appeared harmless, playful, aesthetic, or marginal. The World Wide Web's early association with hobbyist culture, academic sharing, and countercultural imagination provided a period of cultural embedding before its commercial and institutional dimensions became visible. The smartphone's initial framing as a music player and phone — rather than as a surveillance and productivity device — allowed widespread adoption before the full scope of its implications was legible. In both cases, cultural form preceded and enabled capability expansion.

One implication for AI development strategy is counterintuitive: investment in cultural forms — film, game design, educational narrative, local heritage applications — may produce higher long-term adoption stability than equivalent investment in capability benchmarks. This does not mean that capability is irrelevant, but that capability deployed without cultural mediation may generate resistance faster than familiarity, and that this dynamic is structurally predictable from the mimetic framework rather than accidental.

---

## 12. Conclusion

Artificial intelligence is often discussed as a technical revolution. It may be better understood as a civilizational interpretive crisis — a challenge not primarily to productive capacity but to the symbolic frameworks through which societies define human uniqueness, legitimate achievement, and meaningful work.

The key obstacle to adoption is not necessarily capability, safety, or utility alone, but the symbolic destabilization generated by systems capable of participating in traditionally human cognitive domains. Under such conditions, direct persuasion through demonstrations of superiority intensifies resistance rather than reducing it, because it supplies the inputs for the DRSJ cycle of interpretive stabilization rather than preventing its activation.

This paper proposes an alternative: cultural mediation, emotional embedding, and mimetic desynchronization as a five-mechanism practical framework, measurable through a proposed Mimetic Threat Index and testable against the comparative case analysis of three cinematic AI framings. The framework predicts that the future of AI adoption will depend not only on computation itself but on the cultural forms through which societies learn to coexist with increasingly powerful cognitive tools — and that a children's animated film produced before the AI moment may reveal more about the path to stable adoption than the benchmark communication strategies that currently dominate the field.

This paper is simultaneously a contribution to the understanding of AI adoption and a live experiment in the iterative knowledge-production method described in the *Discours de la seconde méthode*. Its limitations are published alongside its arguments. Its version history is its evidence. Its invitation to object is structural. The next version will be published when the corpus demonstrates sufficient internal consistency and when at least one external reviewer — unfamiliar with the production context — has engaged with its central causal claim.

*Cogentia ergo scimus.*
