## A Situated Micro-Experiment in Commons Governance

## Abstract

This working paper presents the "Republic of Donkeys," an ongoing, place-based experiment in commons governance conducted in Corte, Corsica. The project tests whether functional collective coordination can emerge from minimal, egalitarian structures — without centralized authority, complex institutional design, or speculative incentives.

The experiment combines three elements rarely treated as a unified system: a heuristic device (the Corsican donkey, *u sumeru corsu*), a small founding group operating under iterative decision-making, and lightweight physical anchors in a specific micro-territory. Its distinctive methodological posture — the researcher as embedded practitioner, locally legible as a semi-retired slow-tourism guide — allows access to informal coordination dynamics that institutional research typically cannot reach.

Early observations confirm that physical and living anchoring, narrative simplicity, and minimal rulesets are stronger predictors of coordination success than formal rule complexity. The paper argues that this configuration constitutes a reproducible *governance prototype* — a minimum viable institution testable before any attempt at scaling.

**Keywords:** commons governance, subsidiarity, prefigurative politics, Corsica, place-based experimentation, slow tourism, distributed coordination

## 1. Introduction

Modern governance systems fail locally not for lack of rules, but for excess of them. Institutions designed at distance produce alienation rather than coordination. Communities capable of managing their own commons are displaced by frameworks that cannot see them.

This paper presents an alternative starting point: governance as a practice that emerges from the smallest viable unit, tested under real conditions, validated by function rather than theory.

The Republic of Donkeys is not a metaphor. It is an experiment in progress — one more episode, deliberately modest in form, in the perennial struggle between local self-determination and centralized authority.

## 2. Conceptual Framework

### 2.1 Main Traditions

The project draws selectively on:

- **Commons governance** (Ostrom, 1990) — particularly the design principles for self-governing institutions and the conditions under which communities successfully manage shared resources without privatization or state control
- **Subsidiarity and polycentricity** (Ostrom, 2010) — decisions made at the lowest effective level, with higher levels serving only what lower levels cannot manage
- **Prefigurative politics** (Yates, 2015) — the practice of enacting, in the present, the social relations one seeks to generalize
- **Place-based experimentation** — territorial specificity as a resource rather than a constraint
- **Institutional ethnography** — the researcher as participant, not distant observer

These serve as orienting references, not rigid frameworks. Real-world functionality takes priority over theoretical elegance.

### 2.2 The Donkey as Heuristic, Material, and Political Device

The Corsican donkey (*u sumeru corsu*) is not incidental to the experiment. It operates simultaneously on four interconnected levels.

**Patrimonial and cultural.** In Corsica, the donkey carries an immense emotional charge. Less than 2% of the active population works in agropastoral activities today, yet this heritage remains a powerful identity marker. Preserving the Corsican donkey is, in a very real sense, an attempt to help preserve the Corsican soul — a living link to a memory that industrial and mass-tourism models have systematically eroded. This dimension generates immediate sympathy and local legitimacy that no institutional label can replicate.

**Material and biological.** As a real animal on site, the donkey imposes rhythms and constraints that stabilize group dynamics. It creates obligations that participants tend to honor more consistently than abstract procedural commitments. The donkey does not vote — but it stabilizes.

**Epistemic and egalitarian.** The donkey knows nothing of rank, credential, or reputation. Its presence neutralizes the implicit hierarchies that routinely distort human deliberation. Around a donkey, people tend to speak more directly, with less performance. This is the donkey's most underappreciated governance function.

**Anti-messianic and political.** The donkey is the anti-hero of Western symbolic tradition — patient, stubborn, occasionally brilliant, structurally resistant to charismatic capture. As a governance symbol, it encodes a deliberate design principle: the system must function *without* a necessary leader, not *with* a better one. Its presence anchors the experiment in *ascending subsidiarity* — bottom-up legitimacy as a structural counter to both Parisian centralism and local elected officials who resist genuine transparency and power-sharing.

Animal and human well-being are treated as a single, non-negotiable constraint.

The specific animal in this experiment — *Rossignol*, a Corsican donkey based in Corte — is not a generic stand-in. Its name (nightingale), its breed, its territory are constitutive elements of the system's identity.

## 3. Core Hypothesis

> A small-scale, locally embedded, non-speculative organizational system — based on participant equality, physical and living anchoring, and iterative rule formation — can generate functional governance outcomes without centralized control, at lower cost and higher adaptability than equivalent formal institutions.

This hypothesis rests on a foundational postulate: political equality, despite its imperfections and inherent risks, remains the model least prone to capture by a minority at the expense of the general population. The experiment accepts this postulate as an ethical constraint, not merely an analytical assumption.

## 4. Methodology

### 4.1 Embedded Research Posture

The researcher occupies the position of *participant-initiator* — present in the field, recognized locally not as an analyst but as a practitioner. This posture is deliberate: it minimizes the observer effect and maximizes access to informal coordination dynamics that institutional research cannot reach.

The practitioner identity — a semi-retired individual engaged in slow tourism and guided walks with a donkey — is not a deception but an *epistemic simplification*. It presents the experiment at the level of abstraction legible to its participants without foreclosing more rigorous analysis at a second level. It also constitutes a field test of a specific governance hypothesis: that the most effective entry point into dense, closed social networks is not institutional authority but recognized harmlessness.

### 4.2 Approach

- *In situ* experimentation under natural social conditions
- Small founding group (initial triad) with fluid roles
- Iterative decision cycles — no predetermined endpoint
- Participant-observation with reflexive documentation
- Deliberate constraints: limited scale, partial disclosure, reversibility preserved

### 4.3 Validation Logic

- Functional outcomes over theoretical consistency
- Incremental validation before any scaling
- Local legibility as a primary success indicator
- Continuation contingent on demonstrated real-world robustness

## 5. Initial Configuration

**Location.** Corte, Corsica — a small city of approximately 8,000 inhabitants (up to 15,000 seasonally including the university), located at the geographic center of the island. Characterized by coexistence of institutional, academic, and rural social structures; high density of informal networks relative to population size; strong place-identity and historical resonance.

**Participants.** Three founding participants with distinct competency profiles. Roles emergent rather than assigned. Fully shared decision-making capacity from the outset. Deliberate inclusion of participants with different relationships to local institutional power.

**Material and living anchors.** Land access points and walking routes across the micro-territory; minimal technical infrastructure (shelter, energy, mobility); *Rossignol* — one Corsican donkey, the system's primary non-human participant.

**Provisional revenue model.** Dual source: visitors interested in slow and place-based tourism; locals with storage, mobility, or service needs. Revenue model is functional, not speculative.

---

## 6. Operational Principles

The experiment operates under a minimal, explicitly revisable ruleset:

1. **Equality in deliberation** — no participant holds permanent authority
2. **Ascending subsidiarity** — decisions made at the lowest effective level; higher levels serve only what lower levels cannot
3. **Non-speculation** — resources used for function, not accumulation
4. **Use-right priority** — access over ownership wherever possible
5. **Iterative rule formation** — rules emerge from observed friction, not anticipation
6. **Reversibility** — no decision is irreversible by design at this stage

## 7. Preliminary Observations

At the current early stage, the following patterns have emerged:

**Coordination arises faster than expected** when participants share physical space and a common material responsibility — the donkey, the route, the land.

**Informal social mapping** — the guided walk format generates organic access to micro-territorial networks inaccessible through formal institutional channels. Moving through a territory with a donkey is a form of slow network cartography.

**Narrative coherence** — the "republic" framing creates internal cohesion and external legibility simultaneously, without requiring formal legal structure. The story the system tells about itself shapes its behavior as much as its formal rules.

**Trust as a by-product** — repeated, low-stakes interactions in naturalistic settings accumulate relational capital more efficiently than structured meetings.

**Constraint as governance** — local physical, legal, and social constraints shape behavior more effectively than abstract procedural rules.

These observations remain provisional and context-specific.

## 8. Discussion

### 8.1 Limits

Very small initial sample; no statistical validity claimed. High dependency on founding participant alignment. Long-term sustainability untested. Legal ambiguity around informal governance structures in French territorial law. The experiment remains fragile and depends substantially on the initiator's capacity to navigate social, legal, and economic obstacles.

### 8.2 Productive Tensions

**Informality vs. legibility.** The experiment must remain legible to external observers without being formalized prematurely. Legal structuring (cooperative forms, associations) will be explored only after demonstrated robustness.

**Narrative vs. analysis.** The donkey frame aids engagement and generates sympathy, but carries the risk of romanticization and risks trivializing the governance experiment in academic contexts. This tension is managed through deliberate register separation between public narrative and analytical documentation.

**Opacity vs. transparency.** Operational details are partially withheld to protect participants and system integrity. This is in tension with the experiment's own commitment to transparency — a tension acknowledged rather than resolved.

**Embedded posture vs. analytical distance.** The participant-initiator position generates rich data but requires ongoing reflexivity to avoid confirmation bias.

### 8.3 The Role of Non-Human Participants

The inclusion of *Rossignol* as a genuine system component — not merely a symbol — raises underexplored questions in commons governance theory. Non-human participants create obligations that human participants honor more consistently than abstract procedural commitments. The animal's biological needs impose a rhythm on the group that no governance document can replicate. This finding, modest as it appears, suggests that the material composition of a governance system — including its non-human members — deserves more systematic attention than current frameworks provide.

---

## 9. Scaling Implications

Scaling will only be considered after multiple successful iterations. No generalization is claimed at this stage.

If the micro-scale configuration proves stable, possible developments include:

**Commune-level diffusion** — the model could be adapted to other Corsican communes, with each local context requiring its own iteration rather than replication.

**Mule path reopening** — historic transhumance routes across Corsica represent a dormant commons infrastructure. Their reactivation as slow-tourism and agropastoral corridors would extend the experiment's territorial logic without requiring centralized coordination.

**Slow-life network** — a distributed network of donkey-anchored commons nodes could constitute an alternative to mass tourism infrastructure, offering a model of territorial development that serves residents rather than rentier interests.

**Governance seed methodology** — the Republic of Donkeys is, in this sense, a *governance prototype*: not a model to replicate, but a method to iterate. The sequence — minimum viable institution first, physical anchoring as non-optional, narrative as infrastructure — may generalize beyond this specific context.

## 10. Ethical and Disclosure Considerations

This document intentionally omits precise asset locations and sensitive operational details to protect participants and system integrity. Personal data is excluded. Public narrative is separated from operational detail. Participant anonymity is maintained at this stage of publication.

---

## 11. Conclusion

The Republic of Donkeys proposes that governance can be prototyped — that institutions need not be fully designed before they are partially lived. It offers a deliberately humble entry point into questions of collective self-determination: a man, a donkey, a small territory, and a minimal set of shared principles.

Whether this seed can grow into something institutionally durable remains an open empirical question. What the early evidence suggests is that the conditions for functional coordination are less exceptional than governance theory typically assumes — and that the most consequential governance innovations may begin not in conference rooms, but on walking paths.

The experiment is ongoing. Observations are provisional. Participation is open to those who show up.
