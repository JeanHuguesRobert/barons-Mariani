# Markets of Usage Transitions in Multi-Use Physical Assets
## Toward a Theory of Friction-Based Economic Value

**Author:** Jean Hugues Noël Robert, baron Mariani  
**Affiliation:** Institut Mariani, 1 cours Paoli, F-20250 Corte, Corse  
**Contact:** jhr@baronsmariani.org

## Abstract

This paper proposes a unifying conceptual framework for a class of economic systems in which value is not primarily generated by asset usage, but by the management of transitions between incompatible usage states of multi-purpose physical assets.

We argue that these "transition markets" are systematically under-identified in existing economic literature due to their diffusion across multiple disciplines (transaction cost economics, urban economics, logistics, and platform theory). We introduce a minimal formal structure, a typology of friction-based value creation systems, and a critical positioning relative to three established theoretical traditions (Coase/Williamson, Rochet/Tirole, Ostrom).

A case study anchored in a constrained insular university town is used as the empirical reference throughout.

**Separation of registers:** this paper operates simultaneously in three registers that are linked but non-fusible: economic analysis, which describes cost structures and market failures; political analysis, which addresses governance choices that precede and condition markets; and institutional analysis, which identifies the organizational forms that can resolve what neither the market nor the state can spontaneously produce.

---

## 1. Introduction

Classical economic theory primarily focuses on:

- allocation of resources across competing uses
- optimization of asset utilization rates
- matching of supply and demand
- minimization of idle capacity

However, in many real-world systems, a significant portion of economic loss and opportunity arises not from inefficient usage, but from **inefficient transitions between usage states**.

Examples include:

- residential housing transitioning between student tenancy and tourist rental
- industrial equipment reconfiguration between production cycles
- seasonal infrastructure (tourism, events, agriculture)
- logistics buffers between production and distribution stages
- computing infrastructure reallocation between workload types

These systems share a structural property:

> value leakage occurs in the *interstitial phase between two optimized states of use*.

This paper formalizes this class of systems and positions the framework within and against existing theoretical traditions.

---

## 2. Conceptual Problem

Let an asset $A$ have multiple possible usage states $S_1, S_2, ..., S_n$.

Transitions between states $T_{ij}$ incur:

- time cost
- coordination cost
- reconfiguration cost
- opportunity cost of idle time

Standard economic models focus on optimizing:

$$U(S_i): \text{utility of each usage state}$$

This paper focuses on:

$$C(T_{ij}): \text{cost of transition between states}$$

We argue that in many constrained systems, $C(T_{ij})$ is economically significant and systematically under-optimized, and that the infrastructure required to reduce it is systematically under-provided.

---

## 3. Definitions

### Definition 1 — Transition Market

A transition market is a system in which economic value is generated by reducing or reorganizing the cost associated with transitions between incompatible usage states of a shared physical asset.

### Definition 2 — Frictional Value

Frictional value is the economic value created by reducing: idle time between usages; coordination overhead between agents; and physical reconfiguration costs.

### Definition 3 — Phase Specificity

Phase specificity is the degree to which the value of an asset is conditioned on its position in a usage cycle, independently of its intrinsic physical properties. This concept extends Williamson's typology of asset specificity (site, physical, human, dedicated) by adding a temporal and cyclical dimension.

---

## 4. Structural Properties

Transition markets exhibit four structural properties.

### 4.1 Diffusion of Costs

Transition costs are distributed across multiple agents and are not aggregated into any single decision-making unit. No individual agent bears a share sufficient to justify investment in transition infrastructure alone.

### 4.2 Fractal Structure

Transition costs exist at multiple scales simultaneously:

- micro: individual relocation or reconfiguration
- meso: seasonal rental or production cycles
- macro: urban, regional, or sectoral cycles

The same structural problem recurs self-similarly across scales.

### 4.3 Temporal Asymmetry

Transitions are time-bound and synchronized with external cycles (academic calendars, tourism seasons, industrial schedules) that are largely exogenous to the agents involved.

### 4.4 Non-Substitutability of Transition Time

Unlike usage time, transition time cannot be eliminated through optimization of usage — it can only be reorganized, compressed, or institutionally absorbed.

---

## 5. Mechanisms of Value Capture

Five primary mechanisms reduce transition costs:

### 5.1 Buffering

Temporary storage of physical assets, persons, or goods during the interstitial phase.

### 5.2 Standardization

Reduction of heterogeneity in transition units, enabling faster and cheaper reconfiguration (containers, modular systems, standardized processes).

### 5.3 Temporal Matching

Algorithmic or institutional alignment of supply and demand across incompatible cycles.

### 5.4 Gatekeeping of Transitions

Control of access points where transitions occur — logistical or institutional bottlenecks that can be monetized or governed.

### 5.5 Integration into Behavioral Routines

Embedding transition services into the habitual decision processes of agents, reducing the activation energy required to use them.

---

## 6. Case Study: Seasonal Academic–Tourism Housing System

A prototypical transition market is observed in small university towns with strong seasonal tourism. The system presents the following characteristics:

- annual academic cycle with partial early departure of students
- high-value seasonal tourism demand concentrated in summer months
- rigid and heterogeneous housing stock with limited adaptability
- individual agents bearing transition costs (moving, storage, cleaning, reletting) without coordination

Observed inefficiencies:

- vacancy periods between tenant cycles
- repeated relocation costs borne by students
- fragmented storage needs unaddressed by the market
- coordination costs externalized to individual landlords and tenants

This system exhibits all four structural properties identified above. It also exhibits a fifth property specific to insular or geographically constrained contexts: the market is structurally too small to attract spontaneous private investment in transition infrastructure, producing a stable inefficient equilibrium.

## 7. Relation to Existing Literature

The present framework intersects with multiple established traditions while maintaining a distinct theoretical contribution relative to each.

### 7.1 Transaction Cost Economics (Coase 1937, Williamson 1975, 1985)

Transaction Cost Economics (TCE) is the natural reference point. Coase identifies the costs of using the market as the foundation of the firm; Williamson refines this around three dimensions: asset specificity, bounded rationality, and opportunism.

We share with TCE the postulate that non-usage frictions are theoretically underweighted. However, a structural divergence is necessary:

> **Transaction costs are costs of contractualization around a usage state.  
> Transition costs are physical and temporal costs between two usage states.**

This distinction is not terminological. In a Williamsonian world with zero contracting costs, transition costs persist. They are **pre-contractual**: they exist independently of any agency relationship, information asymmetry, or possibility of opportunism. A housing unit that sits vacant between tenants is not awaiting a contract — it is awaiting physical reconfiguration and temporal coordination.

Formally, let:

- $C_{tc}$: transaction costs (contracting, negotiation, enforcement)
- $C_{tr}$: transition costs (reconfiguration, delay, intertemporal coordination)

Williamson addresses $C_{tc}$; this paper addresses $C_{tr}$. These two classes are **orthogonal**: reducing one does not imply reducing the other.

Definition 3 above (phase specificity) represents a fifth dimension of asset specificity, absent from Williamson's typology, that determines the structure and intensity of transition markets.

### 7.2 Platform Economics (Rochet & Tirole 2003, Parker & Van Alstyne 2005)

Multi-sided platform economics offers a second reference. Platforms create value by reducing matching costs between populations of agents whose utilities are complementary. The critical assumption is that usage states are *compatible*: the platform does not manage the transition itself, it manages the meeting.

The distinction is precise:

> **Standard platforms presuppose compatibility of usage states between parties.  
> Transition markets manage discontinuities between incompatible usage states.**

Airbnb is not a transition market — it is a matching market between a host (state: accommodation available) and a traveler (state: seeking accommodation). Compatibility of states is presupposed. The platform externalizes the reconfiguration between an annual student tenant and a summer tourist onto the landlord, generating precisely the frictions this paper identifies.

A genuine transition market would internalize: physical reconfiguration; the vacancy window; storage of personal effects; and coordination with external cycles (academic calendar, tourist season). No existing platform addresses this combination. This constitutes an **unoccupied market space**, structurally distinct from matching platforms.

### 7.3 Division of Labor and Market Size (Stigler 1951)

Stigler's development of the Smithian thesis demonstrates that specialization — and therefore the existence of dedicated markets — is only viable above a market size threshold. Below this threshold, specialized services are internalized by generalist entities or simply not provided.

This explains structurally why transition markets are systematically **underdeveloped in small economies**:

- demand for transition services is real but diffuse
- the addressable market is insufficient to justify dedicated infrastructure
- agents internalize transition costs individually at high unit cost

This Stiglerian argument generates a testable prediction: formalized transition markets should emerge first in large metropolitan areas with high density of usage cycles, and remain absent or informal in small towns with intense cyclical usage (insular university towns, alpine stations, mid-sized coastal resorts). This prediction is empirically observable and motivates institutional responses beyond spontaneous market formation.

### 7.4 Collective Action and Public Goods (Olson 1965, Demsetz 1964)

Transition costs exhibit a structural property directly relevant to collective action theory: they are **diffuse** — borne by many agents individually, with no single agent bearing a share sufficient to justify infrastructure investment.

If $N$ agents each bear a transition cost $c_i$, and an infrastructure investment would reduce this to $c_i'$, the aggregate benefit $\sum(c_i - c_i')$ may be economically significant while no individual perceives $c_i - c_i'$ as sufficient to justify unilateral investment.

This is an **Olsonian market failure**: the under-provision of transition infrastructure is structural, not conjunctural. The standard solutions — privatization (a single actor internalizes diffuse demand) or public regulation (the collective provides the good) — do not exhaust the solution space. A third path exists, which requires separate analysis.

## 8. Original Contribution: A Topology of Transition Markets

The following table positions the present framework within the existing theoretical landscape:

| Dimension | TCE (Williamson) | Platforms (Rochet–Tirole) | This paper |
|---|---|---|---|
| Central object | Contracting cost | Matching cost | Inter-state transition cost |
| Nature of friction | Informational / behavioral | Informational / two-sided | Physical / temporal |
| Assumption on states | Given | Compatible | Incompatible |
| Institutional solution | Firm vs. market | Two-sided platform | Transition infrastructure |
| Characteristic failure | Hold-up, asset specificity | Chicken-and-egg, network externalities | Diffusion, Stigler threshold |

The present framework does not compete with these traditions: it **completes them on a shared blind spot**. TCE does not address inter-contractual phases. Platforms externalize transitions. Public goods theory has not formalized the transition phase as an autonomous economic object.

The concept of **phase specificity** (Definition 3) is the minimal original addition required to articulate these three traditions into a unified account of transition market failures.

## 9. Propositions

**Proposition 1 — Cost significance**  
In multi-use constrained asset systems, transition costs can represent a significant fraction of total economic inefficiency, independently of contracting costs and matching costs.

**Proposition 2 — Structural under-provision**  
Markets systematically under-provide dedicated infrastructure for transitions due to the diffusion and fragmentation of demand below the Stigler viability threshold.

**Proposition 3 — First-mover advantage and behavioral lock-in**  
Because transitions follow repetitive external cycles (academic, seasonal, industrial), the first actor to integrate transition management into the habitual decision processes of agents acquires a persistent structural advantage — analogous to platform network effects, but grounded in behavioral integration rather than network density.

**Proposition 4 — Viability threshold**  
A formalized transition market emerges spontaneously only if aggregate demand for transition services exceeds a threshold determined by the fixed cost structure of the required infrastructure. Below this threshold, costs are internalized by agents or left unaddressed.

**Proposition 5 — Predictive geography**  
Spontaneous transition markets should be empirically absent in small-economy contexts with high phase specificity and strong cyclical usage patterns, regardless of the intensity of the underlying transition inefficiency.

**Proposition 6 — Governance as political decision**  
See Section 10.

## 10. Governance as a Distinct and Political Dimension

### 10.1 Why governance requires separate analysis

Propositions 1 through 5 operate within the register of economic analysis: they describe cost structures, market failures, viability thresholds, and dynamic advantages. Proposition 6 introduces a deliberate change of register.

> **The question of *who* manages transition infrastructure is not resolvable by economic analysis alone. It is a political decision.**

This section develops three arguments for this position.

### 10.2 The commons as a third way (Ostrom 1990)

Standard theory offers two responses to Olsonian collective action failure: privatization (one actor internalizes diffuse demand) or public regulation (the collective provides the public good). Elinor Ostrom's empirical work demonstrated that this dichotomy is false: communities sustainably manage common resources without privatization or state intervention, provided specific institutional conditions are met — clear rules, participation of users in their definition, conflict resolution mechanisms, external recognition.

Transition markets in contexts of scarce and cyclical resources satisfy the applicability conditions of the Ostromian framework:

- finite and clearly bounded resource
- identifiable community of users
- predictable and repeated usage cycles
- transition costs collectively borne but diffusely distributed

Commons governance is therefore an analytically grounded option — not an ideological preference.

### 10.3 Why the governance choice is nonetheless political

Ostrom shows that commons governance *can* work. She does not show that it *will be chosen*. That choice depends on:

- the distribution of power between actors (owners, tenants, local government, university)
- the interests of actors in institutional gatekeeping positions
- the political will to recognize and formalize a common where law does not spontaneously provide for it

In France, the instrument of the SCIC (Société Coopérative d'Intérêt Collectif) embodies precisely this recognition: it allows heterogeneous stakeholders (users, local authorities, investors, employees) to be aggregated around an object of collective interest, without recourse to full privatization or direct public management.

But a SCIC does not emerge spontaneously: it requires an initial **coalition decision** among actors whose short-term interests diverge. This decision is irreducibly political — it mobilizes power relations, legitimating narratives, and a deliberate will to refound the local rules of the game.

### 10.4 The irreducibility of the governance decision

For any institutional architecture $G$ managing a transition market, at least one dimension of the choice of $G$ is not determined by economic efficiency criteria:

- two structures may have equivalent allocative efficiency while distributing the transition rent differently among owners, users, and the collective
- the boundary between what is "managed in common" and what remains "private" is not an economic optimum — it is a social convention
- the choice of the perimeter of the common (which assets, which users, which temporal horizon) is a founding act that precedes all optimization

This observation — that governance precedes and conditions the economy, not the inverse — is central to institutional political economy (North 1990, Acemoglu & Robinson 2012). It implies that economic policy papers that ignore the governance dimension produce technically correct but politically inoperable recommendations.

### 10.5 Proposition 6 — Formulation

> **In multi-use asset transition systems within constrained-resource contexts, the choice of governance structure — private, public, or commons-based — is not determined by economic efficiency. It is a political decision that precedes and conditions markets. Economic analysis can illuminate the consequences of each governance choice; it cannot substitute for it.**

The separation of registers stated in the Abstract materializes here: economic analysis defines the problem space; political decision defines the solution space; institutional design implements the chosen solution within that space.

## 11. Implications

This framework implies a shift in economic focus:

From: optimizing usage of assets  
To: optimizing transitions between usage states

This shift reveals a class of "infrastructure-like services" that are neither purely logistical nor purely platform-based, but intermediate coordination systems — systems whose production requires institutional forms that the market alone will not generate below the Stigler threshold, and that the state alone will not produce without political will to recognize the commons.

The practical implication is that territorial actors — municipal governments, universities, cooperative structures, local development agencies — are systematically better positioned than private actors to initiate transition infrastructure, not because they are more efficient, but because they can aggregate diffuse demand, operate below the viability threshold during the initialization phase, and legitimate the governance decision that private actors cannot make unilaterally.

---

## 12. Conclusion

We propose the existence of a previously under-formalized class of economic systems: transition-based value systems in multi-use physical asset environments.

These systems are characterized by: diffusion of costs across agents; fractal repetition of the same structure across scales; temporal synchronization with exogenous cycles; and structural friction that cannot be reduced to contracting or matching costs.

We have positioned this framework against three established theoretical traditions (TCE, platform economics, collective action theory), identifying a shared blind spot — the inter-state transition phase — that none of them formalizes as an autonomous economic object.

We have argued, finally, that the governance of transition infrastructure is a dimension that economic analysis can illuminate but cannot determine: it is a political decision that precedes the economy it makes possible.

## Future Work

- formal modeling of transition cost functions $T_{ij}$ under varying phase specificity conditions
- empirical measurement of transition cost shares relative to total economic loss in seasonal housing markets
- comparative study across cities and sectors: metropolitan vs. insular contexts, academic vs. industrial cycles
- simulation of transition infrastructure efficiency under different governance structures (private, public, SCIC)
- integration with platform economics models: conditions under which a transition market converges toward or diverges from a platform equilibrium
- connection to commons governance literature: extension of Ostrom's design principles to phase-specific resource systems
