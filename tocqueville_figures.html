Figure 1
Aviation Safety vs. Regulatory Intensity, 1970–2022

Left axis (navy): fatal accidents per million flight sectors (IATA Safety Reports; Boeing Statistical Summary 2023). Right axis (orange): stylized index of cumulative aviation safety regulation (ICAO SARPs amendment cycles + FAA major final rules; 1970 = 100). As the accident rate declined fortyfold between 1970 and 2022, regulatory output grew approximately tenfold. The two curves move in strictly opposite directions, illustrating the core prediction of the Generalized Tocqueville Law: tolerance thresholds rise as objective quality improves.

Note: regulatory index is a stylised composite; exact values vary by measurement choice. The directional relationship is robust across alternative specifications.

Figure 2
The Double Bind of Content Moderation, 2010–2023

Left axis (red): NCMEC CyberTipline reports (millions), driven overwhelmingly by automated platform detection systems (NCMEC Annual Reports 2010–2023). Right axis (teal): estimated automated detection rate for policy-violating content — composite index from Meta and YouTube transparency reports. Both series rise together: as detection capability improves, the volume of detected (and therefore reported) harm increases proportionally, generating a progressive intensification of public and regulatory concern caused by — not despite — improving moderation performance. The 160-fold increase in CyberTipline reports between 2010 and 2023 reflects primarily detection improvement, not a corresponding increase in underlying harm.

Figure 3
Co-evolution of Objective Quality Q(t) and Tolerance Threshold τ(t)

Stylised theoretical trajectories. Solid navy: objective quality Q(t), following a logistic improvement curve. Dashed orange: socially constructed tolerance threshold τ(t), which tracks Q(t) upward but adjusts more rapidly as Q approaches its ceiling (convexity condition, Section 3.1). The shaded region is the absolute deficit Δ(t) = τ(t) − Q(t), which narrows slowly in absolute terms. Dotted red: perceived severity index S(t) = Δ(t) / (1 − Q(t)), measuring the deficit relative to the remaining distance to perfection. S(t) increases monotonically even as Δ(t) narrows — the core formal property of the law. Even near-perfect systems are perceived as failing because almost all remaining imperfection lies within the zone where improvement is deemed mandatory.

Figure 4
The Four Constitutive Mechanisms and Their Interactions

The four mechanisms of the Generalized Tocqueville Law and their interactions. Mechanisms M1 and M2 (blue) operate at the normative level, shifting the tolerance threshold upward in response to demonstrated progress. Mechanisms M3 and M4 (orange) operate at the cognitive and comparative level, amplifying the experienced significance of the residual deficit. Together they drive the perceived deficit Δ(t) = τ(t) − Q(t) in a direction that is counterintuitive with respect to the direction of objective improvement. Feedback arrows indicate that a widening perceived deficit reinforces relative frustration (M3) and structural amnesia (M4), creating a self-sustaining dynamic.

Figure 5
The Easterlin Paradox as Individual-Level Confirmation, United States 1972–2020

Left axis (navy): US real GDP per capita, index 1972 = 100 (World Bank / BEA). Right axis (orange): mean life satisfaction score, index 1972 = 100 (World Values Survey; General Social Survey; Gallup World Poll). Over nearly five decades, real GDP per capita more than doubled while reported life satisfaction remained essentially flat. This is the individual psychological expression — the hedonic treadmill (Brickman and Campbell, 1971; Easterlin, 1974) — of the same structural mechanism described at the collective normative level by the Generalized Tocqueville Law. The two phenomena are analytically distinct (individual aspiration adjustment vs. collective normative recalibration) but mechanistically homologous.
