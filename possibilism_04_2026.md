# Possibilism: Notes Toward a Research Program on Possibility Exploration

**Jean Hugues Noël Robert, baron Mariani**

Institut Mariani / C.O.R.S.I.C.A. - 1 cours Paoli, F-20250 Corte.

jhr@baronsmariani.org

Working Paper — April 2026

---

## Abstract

This paper introduces Possibilism as a conceptual research program — not a unified theory — for exploring whether biological evolution, cultural emergence, technological creativity, and collective intelligence may be studied through a shared analogical lens: the open-ended exploration of adjacent possibility spaces.

The framework is formulated as an *analogical transfer hypothesis*, not a claim of structural identity across domains. It draws on Darwinian evolution, dynamical systems theory, complexity science, commons governance, and process philosophy. Its central contribution is threefold: (1) the concept of *terrain configuration* as a model of innovation distinct from linear problem-solving; (2) the *correctability thesis* — that distributed exploration systems are preferable to concentrated ones not because they are more efficient but because their failures are recoverable; and (3) a preliminary taxonomy of pathological attractors, including the specific pathology of *invidia* as an anti-exploratory social dynamic.

The paper explicitly addresses what would falsify or render Possibilism useless, and warns against romanticized readings of possibility exploration. Possibility exploration is compatible with catastrophe. The framework is offered fallibilistically.

**Keywords:** Possibility space; Adjacent possible; Terrain configuration; Attractors; Correctability; Process philosophy; Commons; Emergence; Falsifiability; Pathological attractors.

## 1. Introduction: A Lens, Not a Law

Modern thought often separates cosmology, biology, cognition, ethics, and political organization into disconnected domains. This paper explores whether a common *analogical lens* — the exploration of adjacent possibility spaces — might illuminate each domain without collapsing their differences.

The emphasis on *analogy* is deliberate and load-bearing. Possibilism does not claim that evolution and democracy *are* the same process. It claims that modeling them as expressions of related dynamics may generate transferable insights. The test is pragmatic: does the transfer produce non-trivial predictions or design principles that would not have emerged from within each domain alone?

Gregory Bateson asked: *what is the pattern which connects the crab to the lobster, the orchid to the primrose, and all four of them to me?* (Bateson, 1979). Possibilism is a partial, tentative answer to that question — not at the level of substance, but at the level of process.

The framework begins from a simple observation:

Evolution explores.

Science explores.

Democracy, at its best, explores.

Markets explore.

Distributed computation explores.

Might these be differently scaled, differently substrated expressions of related exploratory dynamics? And if so, what follows for how we design institutions, infrastructure, and governance?

---

## 2. Core Hypothesis: The Analogical Transfer Claim

The working hypothesis of Possibilism is:

> *Reality may be partially modeled as an open process exploring adjacent possible states under material constraints — and this modeling may transfer productively across biological, cognitive, institutional, and technological domains.*

Three qualifications are essential:

**Partial.** The model does not claim to explain everything within any domain. It claims to illuminate the exploratory dimension of each.

**Productive.** The test of the analogy is whether it generates non-trivial predictions or design principles. Sterile analogies — those that merely rename known phenomena — are not contributions.

**Material constraints.** Exploration is not free-floating. It occurs under specific physical, energetic, and institutional constraints that shape which possibilities are adjacent. The constraints are not incidental — they are constitutive of the exploration.

Possibilism differs explicitly from:

- **Technological solutionism:** the assumption that problems have optimal solutions waiting to be found and implemented by sufficiently capable actors.
- **Deterministic reductionism:** the assumption that future states are in principle computable from present states given sufficient information.
- **Naive progress narratives:** the assumption that exploration is biased toward improvement.
- **Grand unification theories:** the claim that a single framework explains all phenomena in all domains. Possibilism makes no such claim.

## 3. Theoretical Lineage: What Possibilism Adds

Possibilism does not emerge from nowhere. Several prior frameworks share its concerns. The contribution of Possibilism must be stated relative to this lineage — otherwise it risks renaming known ideas.

**Stuart Kauffman** (1996; 2019) introduced the "adjacent possible" concept: at each moment, realizable states are constrained by what is currently actualized, and exploration expands the adjacent possible — each new state creates new neighbors. Kauffman applies this primarily to biological and economic evolution. Possibilism extends the framework to institutional and governance design, asking: what are the adjacent possible states of democratic institutions, and what configurations expand vs. foreclose them?

**Elinor Ostrom** (1990) demonstrated empirically that commons-based institutions can govern shared resources sustainably across wildly different cultural contexts, provided certain design principles hold. Possibilism reads Ostrom's principles as a *pattern language for institutional possibility exploration* — structural conditions that keep the space of institutional futures open rather than foreclosed by monopoly or tragedy.

**Ilya Prigogine** (1984) developed the concept of dissipative structures: far-from-equilibrium systems that maintain themselves through continuous energy flux and exhibit spontaneous self-organization. Possibilism draws on this but shifts the emphasis from thermodynamic self-organization to *governance of the conditions* under which self-organization occurs.

**Process philosophy** (Whitehead, 1929; Rescher, 1996) treats becoming rather than being as fundamental — reality is constituted by processes, not static substances. Possibilism is implicitly processual: it treats systems as ongoing explorations rather than fixed states. The contribution beyond process philosophy is the specific focus on the *topology* of possibility spaces and the *governance* of their expansion or contraction.

**Gregory Bateson** (1979) proposed that living systems share a "pattern which connects" — not at the level of substance but at the level of relationship, structure, and process. His concept of *deutero-learning* (learning to learn) anticipates what Possibilism calls reflexive evolution. His emphasis on context and relationship as primary is the methodological ancestor of terrain configuration theory.

**Herbert Simon** (1969) analyzed *the sciences of the artificial* — designed systems that achieve goals through heuristic search in problem spaces. Possibilism extends Simon's framework by questioning whether problem spaces are given or configured, and by arguing that the most consequential design choices concern the topology of the search space itself, not the search algorithm.

**What Possibilism adds:** The conjunction of these lineages around a specific design-relevant question — *who configures the terrain of exploration, and with what consequences for which futures remain reachable?* — is the specific contribution. This question is not answered by any of the individual frameworks above, because each treats the topology of possibility as given rather than as a design variable subject to governance.

## 4. Three Core Concepts

### 4.1 Terrain Configuration

In the linear model of innovation, problems exist, innovators find solutions, solutions are implemented. The innovator is an agent; the problem and solution are objects. Success is measured by proximity to a predetermined goal.

In the terrain configuration model, innovation is structural: an actor modifies the landscape of adjacent possibilities, enabling solutions to emerge through the distributed exploration of many agents. The innovator is a gardener, not an engineer. Success is measured by the expansion of reachable futures.

The distinction matters practically. A terrain configurator does not need to know the optimal solution — they need to know which landscape configurations keep multiple solutions reachable. This is a different competence, a different institutional role, and a different evaluation criterion.

Terrain configuration theory makes a testable prediction: *innovations that reconfigure possibility landscapes will have larger and longer-lasting effects than innovations that solve specific problems within existing landscapes.* This prediction can in principle be tested historically — by comparing the impact trajectories of landscape-reconfigurations (the printing press, packet-switched networking, the CECA coal-steel pooling agreement) with problem-solutions (specific technological optimizations within existing landscapes).

### 4.2 The Correctability Thesis

Under radical uncertainty — when the correct attractor cannot be identified in advance — a governance preference for *distributed over concentrated* exploration follows from a specific asymmetry:

- A distributed exploration system that converges on a suboptimal attractor can be corrected: other nodes continue exploring, information about the suboptimal attractor accumulates, and governance mechanisms can redirect.
- A concentrated exploration system that converges on a pathological attractor cannot be corrected from within: the actor controlling the concentration point determines both the direction of exploration and the evaluation of outcomes.

This is not an efficiency argument for distribution. It is a *correctability argument*. Distributed systems are preferred not because they find better attractors faster, but because their failures are recoverable and their failures are visible.

The correctability thesis is falsifiable: if distributed exploration systems empirically show lower resilience to pathological attractor lock-in than concentrated ones — controlling for initial conditions — the thesis fails.

### 4.3 Pathological Attractors and Invidia

Possibilism explicitly rejects the view that exploration is biased toward generative attractors. Systems can explore their way — through locally rational steps, each individually defensible — into stable configurations that are deeply harmful.

A taxonomy of pathological attractors:

**Monopoly attractors:** configurations in which a single actor accumulates sufficient control over a shared resource to determine the terms of access for all others. Locally rational (for the monopolist); globally harmful (for the commons).

**Epistemic closure attractors:** configurations in which a belief system becomes self-sealing — able to absorb contrary evidence as confirmation. Science, religious institutions, and political movements can all reach this attractor through locally rational updating steps.

**Civilizational lock-in attractors:** configurations in which a technological or institutional path becomes self-reinforcing to the point where alternative paths are effectively foreclosed — not by prohibition but by accumulated infrastructure, sunk costs, and dependent populations.

**Invidia attractors** deserve specific attention. Invidia — the active hostility to the success of others, distinct from envy (which desires what others have) — is an anti-exploratory social dynamic. Where envy can coexist with exploration (the envious actor may explore in order to acquire what they covet), invidia actively forecloses the exploration of others. Institutions that generate invidia as a stable equilibrium — through zero-sum status competition, punitive leveling, or structural resentment of innovation — are pathological attractors for collective possibility exploration.

Invidia is undertheorized in the economics and governance literature. Its inclusion in Possibilism is an original contribution: it names a specific mechanism through which social systems convert generative exploration into collective foreclosure.

## 5. Reflexive Evolution

Human cognition may represent a specific phase transition in the exploration process: the emergence of agents capable of modeling the exploration process itself, and of using those models to navigate more effectively.

Not outside nature.

Nature reflecting upon itself.

Bateson's *deutero-learning* — learning to learn, acquiring the capacity to modify one's own learning strategies — is the cognitive expression of this transition. Science is institutionalized deutero-learning: a social system organized to systematically improve its own methods of inquiry. Democratic governance, at its best, is institutionalized deutero-governance: a social system organized to systematically improve its own methods of collective decision.

Reflexivity does not guarantee progress. A self-modeling system can model itself into a pathological attractor as effectively as a non-reflexive one. The difference is the potential for recognition and correction — which is only realized if the structural conditions for correction are maintained. Those conditions are not automatic. They must be designed. This is the connection between Possibilism as a philosophical framework and the governance question of who configures the terrain.

## 6. Against Romanticization

Possibilism is vulnerable to a specific misreading: the romantic interpretation, in which exploration is inherently creative, possibility spaces are inherently expansive, and the exploratory dynamic tends toward flourishing.

This reading is wrong, and this section explicitly rejects it.

**Exploration is compatible with catastrophe.** The evolutionary exploration of the Permian-Triassic boundary produced the largest mass extinction in Earth's history — the exploration of new metabolic possibilities by anaerobic organisms generated atmospheric oxygen that was toxic to the dominant life forms. Exploration does not protect against catastrophe. It generates catastrophe as readily as flourishing.

**Attractors are not normatively ordered.** The pathological attractors described in Section 4.3 are as stable — often more stable — than generative ones. Monopoly, epistemic closure, and civilizational lock-in are not aberrations from the exploratory dynamic; they are expressions of it. The exploration converges on a stable configuration; the stability is the problem.

**Adjacent possible expansion is not always good.** The development of nuclear weapons expanded the adjacent possible of human destructive capacity. The development of social media expanded the adjacent possible of coordinated epistemic manipulation. Expanding the space of what is possible is not normatively positive — it is normatively neutral. What matters is which possibilities are explored and which are foreclosed.

**The correctability thesis is not a guarantee.** Distributed systems can maintain correctability under moderate stress. Under extreme stress — civilizational-scale crises, rapid capability growth outpacing governance adaptation — even distributed systems may converge on pathological attractors faster than correction mechanisms can operate.

Possibilism is a framework for thinking clearly about exploration. It is not a theory of progress, a justification of optimism, or a replacement for normative judgment about which attractors are worth pursuing. The normative work must be done separately, and it cannot be derived from the framework.

## 7. Failure Modes of Possibilism

What would show that Possibilism is false, or useless?

**FM-P1: Failure of productive analogy.** If the analogical transfer from evolutionary biology to institutional design generates no non-trivial predictions — if every claim that Possibilism makes about institutions can be derived more directly from existing institutional theory without the evolutionary analogy — then the framework adds no value. It becomes a relabeling exercise.

*Test:* Does terrain configuration theory generate predictions about institutional evolution that differ from predictions made by standard institutional economics or organizational theory? If not, Possibilism is ornamental.

**FM-P2: Falsification of the correctability thesis.** If empirical analysis shows that concentrated exploration systems (hierarchical organizations, centralized states, dominant firms) are as resilient to pathological attractor lock-in as distributed ones — controlling for initial conditions and resource endowments — then the correctability thesis fails, and Possibilism loses its primary governance-relevant claim.

*Test:* Cross-sectional or longitudinal comparison of lock-in rates and recovery rates in distributed vs. concentrated systems across domains (markets, political systems, technological ecosystems).

**FM-P3: Incoherence of the adjacent possible across domains.** If "adjacent possible" cannot be given a coherent formal definition in institutional domains — if the concept is so metaphorical as to resist operationalization — then the framework cannot generate testable predictions and fails the falsifiability criterion.

*Test:* Attempt formal operationalization in at least two non-biological domains. If the operationalizations are mutually inconsistent or empirically vacuous, FM-P3 is confirmed.

**FM-P4: The romanticization trap (self-referential).** If Possibilism itself becomes an epistemic closure attractor — a framework that absorbs contrary evidence as confirmation of its own thesis — it has become a pathological attractor for its own research program. The framework must remain genuinely falsifiable by its own standards.

*Test:* This failure mode cannot be tested externally. It requires ongoing reflexive vigilance by practitioners of the framework — exactly the deutero-learning capacity described in Section 5.

## 8. Open Research Questions

**Formalization:**
- Can "adjacent possible" be formally defined in institutional state spaces in a way that generates empirically testable predictions?
- Is there a spectral theory of conceptual attractors analogous to the spectral theory of dynamical systems attractors?
- Can invidia be formally modeled as an anti-exploratory social dynamic with measurable institutional signatures?

**Empirical:**
- Does terrain configuration theory predict innovation impact trajectories better than standard models of technological change?
- Do distributed governance systems show lower pathological attractor lock-in rates than concentrated ones, across domains and timescales?
- Can Ostrom's commons design principles be reinterpreted as conditions for maintaining exploratory capacity in shared resource systems?

**Philosophical:**
- Is the correctability thesis derivable from more fundamental principles, or is it an independent axiom of Possibilism?
- What is the relationship between Bateson's "pattern which connects" and Kauffman's "adjacent possible"? Are they convergent descriptions of the same underlying structure?
- Does Possibilism require a processual ontology (Whitehead), or can it be expressed within a more standard metaphysical framework?

---

## 9. Conclusion

Possibilism is not a theory.

It is a research orientation — empirical, speculative, and disciplined by the failure modes it identifies.

Its central wager: that the question *who configures the terrain of exploration?* is among the most consequential questions available for governance research in the current moment — and that existing frameworks, by treating the topology of possibility as given rather than designed, systematically underestimate its importance.

The question is not whether exploration occurs.

The question is who configures the terrain.
